Your growth system · a simple guide

Everything we have set up for you, in plain English.

This guide explains what is now running behind your website, what each part does, and the few simple things you can do to get the most from it. You do not need to be technical, and you do not need to manage any of it day to day.

The big picture

What is now working for you.

Four things are now in place, and they work together quietly in the background:

  • A daily blog. Fresh, well-researched articles are published to your website automatically, building your library of useful content and your visibility in search.
  • Slack updates. You get a message every time new articles go live, and a written performance report once a week.
  • Two measurement tools. Google Analytics and Google Search Console are connected to your site, so we can see exactly how it is performing.
  • A weekly report with three recommendations. Each week you receive a plain-English summary of progress and three specific actions to grow your traffic.

You will also receive a separate content plan that lays out the full schedule of what is being published over the coming year.

Slack

Where your updates land.

Slack is a simple messaging app. We use it to send you everything in one tidy place, rather than email. You have two channels (think of them as folders for messages):

  • Your blog channel – the links to each new article as it publishes, daily.
  • Your reports channel – your written performance report, every Sunday at 6pm.

How to log in

  • Download the Slack app from slack.com/downloads (phone or computer), or use it in your browser at slack.com.
  • Sign in by clicking "Log in with Google" and choosing your Gmail account (the same email your invitation was sent to). If you cannot find the invitation, ask Gareth to resend it.
  • Once in, you will see your two channels listed on the left. Click either to read its messages.

Tip: turn on notifications in the Slack app so a new report or article gives you a gentle nudge.

Your measurement tools

Two free tools now watch your website.

These are the two tools every serious website uses. They are now installed and connected to your site, and they are where your weekly report gets its numbers. You can open them any time, but you never have to; the weekly report does the reading for you.

Google Analytics

Who visits your site, and what they do

Google Analytics shows you what happens once people are on your website. In plain terms, it tells you:

  • How many people visited, and whether that is going up.
  • Where they came from: Google, social media, an ad, or typing your address in directly.
  • Which pages they looked at, and how long they stayed.
  • How many took an action that matters, such as sending an enquiry through your contact form.

Open it at analytics.google.com and sign in by clicking "Log in with Google" and choosing your Gmail account. The live "Realtime" view is a fun place to start; it shows people on your site right now.

Google Search Console

How you show up in Google search

Google Search Console shows you what happens before people reach your site, inside Google search itself. It tells you:

  • Which words and questions people search that your site appears for.
  • How often you appear (impressions) and how often people click through (clicks).
  • Your average position, meaning how high up the results you sit.
  • Which of your pages and articles are getting noticed in search.

Open it at search.google.com/search-console and sign in the same way, clicking "Log in with Google" and choosing your Gmail account. It needs a little time to gather data, so the picture gets richer over the first few weeks.

A quick note on privacy: your site asks visitors to accept cookies before any tracking happens, which keeps you on the right side of privacy rules. Visitors who decline are simply not counted.

Your weekly report

What it says, and what to do with it.

Every Sunday at 6pm, a written report arrives in your Slack reports channel. It reads the numbers from Google Analytics and Search Console for the week, compares them to the week before, and explains in plain language how things are going. Then it gives you three specific recommendations to improve your traffic, chosen for your site that week.

Turning a recommendation into a real change

You have two easy options for each recommendation. Both use an AI assistant. We recommend Claude (the next section explains why), though Codex works too. You simply paste the recommendation in and ask it to help.

Option 1: ask it to sense-check the idea first.

"Here is a recommendation from my weekly report: [paste it in]. Please look at my website and tell me, in simple terms, whether this is a good idea and what you would change."

Option 2: ask it to make the change and publish it.

"Here is a recommendation from my weekly report: [paste it in]. Please make this change to my website and publish it live."

If you are ever unsure, use Option 1 first, or send it to Gareth. Nothing breaks by asking a question.

Your tools for making changes

Claude and Codex, simply put.

Claude and Codex are AI assistants you can talk to in plain English. They can write, plan, research, and (this is the powerful part) make and publish changes to your website for you. You do not need to know any code. You only need to describe what you want.

How a change reaches your live website

When you ask the assistant to make a change, here is what happens for you, automatically:

  1. The assistant makes the change and saves it to GitHub, a secure online store that always holds the latest version of your website's files.
  2. Netlify, the service that hosts your site, spots the update and publishes it to your live website, usually within a minute.

So you describe the change in words, and a minute later it is live. GitHub and Netlify run quietly in the background. You do not need to log into them, and Gareth manages them for you.

The Claude desktop app recommended

Claude is the assistant we recommend. Download it at claude.ai/download (or use it in your browser at claude.ai) and sign in by clicking "Log in with Google" and choosing your Gmail account. A few things it is very good at:

  • Projects. You can create a "Project" for a single area of work, for example one for your website. Everything you do inside it stays together, and Claude remembers the context: your brand, your goals, your past recommendations. It is like a tidy, dedicated workspace that does not get mixed up with anything else.
  • Making website changes. Because your website is connected, you can ask Claude to update a page, fix a wording, add a section, or act on a report recommendation, and publish it for you. You describe the change in words; it does the technical part.
  • Everyday help. Drafting emails and proposals, thinking through a client problem, summarising a long document, preparing for a meeting. It is a capable assistant for the whole business, not just the website.

A note on Codex, and our recommendation

You currently use Codex (the assistant from the makers of ChatGPT, at chatgpt.com) to make changes to your site. Codex is perfectly capable, and you are welcome to keep using it. Our honest recommendation, though, is to move this work to Claude, for two simple reasons:

  • One subscription, not two. If your changes and your everyday work both happen in Claude, you are not paying for two separate tools that do the same job.
  • One way of working. A single, consistent process for publishing changes means fewer chances for things to clash or get confused.

If you would rather stay with Codex, that is genuinely fine. The one thing to remember: ask Codex to read the latest version of your site from GitHub before it makes any change, so it always works from what is actually live and not an older copy. Gareth can set that up with you.

You are always in control. These tools ask before doing anything important, and you can ask them to explain first and act second. When in doubt, ask it to review and explain, then decide, or send it to Gareth.
