CRM consulting & selection

Best CRM for ecommerce. What actually determines the right choice.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.1 July 20266 min read

There's no single best CRM for ecommerce: the right choice depends on order volume, channel count and whether you need marketing automation built in. HubSpot and Zoho suit lean single-channel stores, Klaviyo pairs well with Shopify for email-led growth, and Salesforce or Ometria fit multi-channel retailers with complex data needs.

What to weigh up.

"Best CRM for ecommerce" is the wrong question on its own. A CRM that suits a fifteen-person DTC skincare brand on Shopify will not suit a multi-brand retailer running marketplaces, wholesale and a retail arm through the same stack. Before comparing platforms, get honest about four things: order volume, channel count, whether you need marketing automation built into the CRM or run separately, and who on your team will actually use it day to day.

Order volume matters because it decides whether you need real-time sync with your storefront or whether a nightly batch import is fine. A brand doing 200 orders a month can run comfortably on a CRM with a basic Shopify connector. A brand doing 20,000 orders a month needs a platform built to handle that data volume without falling over, and usually needs a dedicated integration layer rather than a native app.

Channel count changes the calculation again. Sell through your own site only, and most CRMs will cope. Add marketplaces, wholesale and a retail point of sale, and you need a CRM, or a CRM paired with a customer data platform, that can unify customer records across all of them without creating a duplicate every time someone buys through a different channel.

The marketing automation question is where most ecommerce brands get the decision wrong. A CRM manages the customer and sales relationship; a marketing automation or email platform sends the campaigns. Some tools, like HubSpot, try to do both. Others, like Klaviyo, are built for ecommerce marketing first and treat CRM functions as secondary. Neither approach is wrong, but you need to know which one you are buying before you sign a contract, not after.

The best CRM for ecommerce: options compared.

These five come up most often in ecommerce CRM shortlists. None of them is universally "the best": each is built for a different combination of scale, channel complexity and marketing need.

HubSpot's free CRM tier is a genuine option for a brand just getting organised: it gives you a contact record, a deal pipeline and basic automation without a licence cost, and the paid Marketing Hub tiers add campaign functionality as you grow. The tradeoff comes at scale, where contact-based pricing on the marketing side can get expensive quickly.

Klaviyo is not really a CRM in the traditional sense; it is a customer data and marketing platform that many ecommerce brands use instead of one. Its strength is turning purchase and browse behaviour into segmented email and SMS flows, and its partnership with Shopify means the data sync is close to real time. If you need a sales pipeline for B2B or wholesale accounts alongside your DTC business, you will still need something else alongside it.

Zoho CRM earns its place on ecommerce shortlists on price. It covers the core CRM functions well and connects to Shopify and WooCommerce, but the ecommerce-specific workflows need more setup than HubSpot or Ometria provide out of the box. For a founder-led brand with a small operations team, that is a fair trade for the lower cost.

Salesforce, through Commerce Cloud paired with Sales or Service Cloud, is built for retailers who need one system managing inventory, customer data and sales across multiple brands or regions. It is rarely the right first CRM. The implementation cost and timeline only make sense once you have outgrown a simpler platform and the complexity is genuinely there to manage.

Ometria sits in a specific niche: retail and fashion brands, usually mid-market and above, that want a single customer view across email, SMS and increasingly in-store data. It is a strong choice if unifying customer data is your main problem, but it assumes you already have someone dedicated to running it.

Which to choose and when.

If you are a single-channel Shopify brand under about 5,000 orders a month and your main gap is organising sales and support conversations, start with HubSpot's free or entry-level tier. If your main gap is email and SMS revenue rather than pipeline management, Klaviyo will earn its cost faster.

If budget is the binding constraint and you run one or two channels, Zoho CRM gives you the core functionality at a lower price, provided someone on your team has the time to configure the ecommerce workflows properly. Left unconfigured, it will underperform any of the alternatives here, not because the platform is weak but because the value sits in the setup.

If you are running multiple brands, multiple channels or a wholesale arm alongside DTC, and the customer data is genuinely fragmented across systems, Salesforce or Ometria are worth the higher cost and longer implementation. Below that level of complexity, both are usually overbuilt for what the business needs.

How Lauren would decide.

When a client asks me to help pick a CRM for an ecommerce business, I do not start with a feature comparison. I start by mapping where the customer data currently lives, how many places it gets duplicated, and where deals or support tickets fall through gaps between systems. The right CRM is the one that closes those specific gaps, not the one with the longest feature list. If that mapping work is where you are stuck, it is exactly the kind of thing I help with through CRM consulting and selection.

A cart abandonment rate that sits above 70% industry-wide, according to Baymard Institute's ongoing research, is a reminder that most of the value in ecommerce customer data lives in behaviour, not just completed orders. Whichever CRM you choose, the deciding factor is whether it can act on that behavioural data automatically, rather than leaving it sitting in a dashboard nobody checks. That is usually where I spend the first few weeks of an engagement: not choosing the platform, but making sure the data flowing into it is complete enough to act on, the same discipline covered in CRM adoption metrics.

Once you have picked a platform, the next decision is how you roll it out without losing momentum in the first ninety days; my CRM implementation project plan covers that in detail. If you are stuck between two or three platforms and cannot tell which one fits, that is usually a sign the underlying requirements have not been mapped yet. Fix that first, and the shortlist narrows on its own.

Common questions.

What is the best CRM for a small ecommerce business?

For a single-channel store under about 5,000 orders a month, HubSpot's free CRM tier or Zoho CRM covers most needs without a large licence cost. HubSpot suits teams wanting CRM and basic marketing together; Zoho suits teams on a tighter budget willing to configure ecommerce workflows themselves.

Is Klaviyo a CRM?

Not in the traditional sense. Klaviyo is a customer data and marketing platform built for email and SMS, with deep native Shopify integration. It handles customer segmentation and campaigns well but has little of the pipeline or deal management a traditional CRM provides, so many brands run it alongside a separate CRM for B2B or wholesale sales.

When does an ecommerce brand need Salesforce or a similar enterprise CRM?

Once you are running multiple brands, multiple sales channels, or a wholesale operation alongside DTC, and customer data is genuinely fragmented across systems. Below that level of complexity, Salesforce Commerce Cloud is usually more implementation cost and time than the business needs.

How much does an ecommerce CRM cost?

It ranges from free, such as HubSpot's entry CRM tier, to enterprise pricing quoted directly by the vendor for platforms like Salesforce Commerce Cloud or Ometria. Mid-market options like Zoho CRM and Klaviyo typically sit on published monthly plans that scale with contacts, orders or send volume, so cost depends heavily on customer list and order volume rather than a flat per-seat fee.

Can I use more than one platform together?

Yes, and many ecommerce brands do: a CRM for sales and support alongside a dedicated marketing platform like Klaviyo for email and SMS. The risk is data drifting out of sync between the two. If you go this route, the integration between them needs the same attention as the platforms themselves.

What is the biggest mistake brands make when choosing an ecommerce CRM?

Choosing based on the feature list rather than the specific gap they are trying to close. A platform with more features is not automatically the right fit, and the wrong choice usually shows up months later as duplicated customer records, unused automation, or a team that has quietly gone back to spreadsheets.
