CRM implementation & selection

A realistic CRM implementation timeline. Not the one in the sales deck.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.3 July 20267 min read

A CRM implementation timeline typically runs four to six weeks for a small, single-team rollout, three to six months for a mid-market business with several departments, and six months to a year or more for an enterprise deployment. The software rarely sets the pace: clean data and internal agreement on how the system should work do.

The short answer. It depends on data, not the software.

Ask a vendor how long a CRM implementation takes and you will usually hear a number that assumes clean data, a single decision-maker and a team that already agrees on how sales should work. In practice, none of those things are guaranteed, which is why the honest range is wider than most sales conversations admit.

For a small business with one sales team, a handful of pipeline stages and reasonably tidy data, four to six weeks from kick-off to a working go-live is realistic. A mid-market business with marketing, sales and customer success all needing something slightly different from the same system usually needs three to six months. An enterprise rollout, multiple business units, legacy systems to retire, and approval chains that touch several departments, routinely takes six months to a year or longer.

Johnny Grow's October 2025 research into CRM outcomes found that seven in ten implementations exceeded their planned timeline by 30% or more, almost half overran by 50% or more, and one in five missed their original date by 100% or more. The pattern behind those numbers is consistent: teams underestimate how long it takes to agree on configuration, and underestimate the data migration work almost every time.

How it works in practice. Five phases, in order.

A CRM implementation that stays on schedule tends to move through the same five phases, in the same order, regardless of company size:

  • Discovery and requirements (1-3 weeks): mapping the current sales process, agreeing pipeline stages, deciding what "done" looks like for each team that will touch the system.
  • Configuration (2-8 weeks): building pipelines, fields, permissions and automations to match what discovery actually surfaced, not a generic template.
  • Data migration (1-6 weeks, run in parallel with configuration): cleaning, de-duplicating and mapping existing records so what goes into the new system is trustworthy from day one.
  • Testing and integration (1-4 weeks): connecting email, calendar and any finance or marketing tools, then testing with real records before anyone relies on the system.
  • Training and go-live (1-2 weeks, plus a supported first month): hands-on training by role, not a single all-hands demo, and a named point of contact for questions in the first few weeks.

Data migration is the phase most often squeezed, and it is the one that should not be. A CRM that goes live with duplicate contacts, missing deal values or inconsistent stage names does not save time, it just moves the cleanup into a system people are now expected to trust for forecasting.

A worked example makes this concrete. A mid-market business with twelve sales users, two integrations and a moderately messy contact database might plan a fourteen-week rollout like this: weeks 1-2 for discovery and stage mapping, weeks 2-8 for configuration running alongside data cleaning, weeks 8-11 for integration and testing with real records, week 12 for a phased go-live to a pilot group of three or four users, and weeks 13-14 for full rollout and role-based training. Building in a pilot week before the full team switches over catches configuration mistakes while the blast radius is still small, which is far cheaper than discovering the same mistake once every rep depends on the system.

What good looks like. The signs a timeline is realistic.

A trustworthy timeline has a few characteristics in common. It names a single, empowered decision-maker for configuration choices, rather than routing every field name through a committee. It treats data migration as a parallel workstream from week one, not a task that starts once the "real" build is finished. It phases the launch: core pipeline and the fields people actually use go live first, with advanced automations, reporting and integrations following in a second release once the team has used the basics for a few weeks.

It also builds in a genuinely supported adoption period, not just a go-live date. The system going live is not the finish line, people actually using it correctly for the first full sales cycle is. Teams that treat go-live as the end of the project tend to see usage drop within weeks, because nobody was there to answer the small questions that come up once people start relying on the tool daily.

Worth asking any implementation partner or internal lead directly: what does the timeline assume about data quality, who has final say on configuration decisions, and what happens in the first thirty days after go-live. Vague answers to any of those three questions are a better predictor of a slipping timeline than the number of weeks quoted in the proposal. A partner who can describe exactly how they will handle a messy contact list or a disagreement between sales and finance over deal stages has clearly done this before; one who glosses over it usually hasn't planned for it.

Pitfalls to avoid. Where good timelines go wrong.

The most common failure mode is not a bad piece of software, it is scope creep disguised as thoroughness: trying to build every automation, every integration and every report before go-live, so the "simple" implementation quietly becomes a six-month project with no working system to show for the first five of them. Launching with the core workflow live and adding complexity afterwards, once real usage tells you what is actually needed, almost always beats trying to design it all up front.

The second common failure is skipping proper stakeholder alignment before configuration starts. If sales, marketing and finance have not agreed what a "qualified lead" or a "closed-won" deal actually means before the system is built, that disagreement resurfaces during testing, and resolving it then costs far more time than resolving it in week one would have. Every week spent on discovery and alignment upfront tends to save two or three weeks later in rework.

The third is treating training as an afterthought scheduled for the last day of the project. A CRM that nobody was properly trained on gets used inconsistently, which corrupts the reporting that was the point of implementing it in the first place. Build training and a supported first month into the plan from the start, and budget for it the same way you would budget for the configuration work itself.

A fourth, quieter pitfall is choosing the wrong implementation partner or internal owner for the size of the job. A small business rollout run by someone with genuine spare capacity and decision-making authority moves fast. The same rollout assigned to someone juggling it alongside their day job, with no authority to make configuration calls without checking with three other people, will drift regardless of how good the software is. Timeline estimates assume a properly resourced project; resourcing it properly is usually the single biggest lever a business actually controls.

Common questions.

How long does a CRM implementation actually take?

For a small, single-team rollout with reasonably clean data, four to six weeks from kick-off to go-live is realistic. A mid-market business with several departments, custom pipelines and a couple of integrations usually needs three to six months. An enterprise rollout with multiple business units, legacy data and complex approval chains routinely runs six months to a year or more.

What's the biggest reason CRM implementations run over schedule?

Underestimating data migration and underestimating how long it takes people to agree on how the CRM should actually be configured. Johnny Grow's October 2025 research on CRM failure rates found seven in ten implementations exceeded their planned timeline by 30% or more, and one in five missed it by 100% or more, with data quality and stakeholder disagreement the recurring culprits.

Can a CRM implementation timeline be compressed?

Yes, within limits. The fastest lever is scoping down what goes live on day one: launch with the core pipeline and the fields the team actually uses, then add automations, integrations and reporting in a second phase. Trying to build the finished, fully automated system before go-live is the most common cause of a timeline that keeps slipping.

Should data migration happen before or during the main build?

Start data cleaning in parallel with the configuration work, not after it. Migrating messy data into a newly built CRM just moves the mess into a more expensive system and forces rework once someone notices duplicate records or missing fields during testing. Clean, de-duplicate and map the data while the CRM structure is being built, then migrate once, close to go-live.

How much time should be set aside for user training within the timeline?

Budget at least one to two weeks of hands-on training and a supported first month after go-live, not a single onboarding session. Adoption, not the software going live, is what determines whether the implementation was worth doing, and teams that skip proper training tend to revert to spreadsheets within the first quarter.
