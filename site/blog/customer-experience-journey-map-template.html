Customer journey mapping

A customer experience journey map template. Stages, evidence, one page.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.17 July 20266 min read

A customer experience journey map template plots five stages, awareness, consideration, decision, onboarding, and ongoing use and renewal, against what the customer does, feels and needs at each one, built from real evidence rather than assumption. Fill it in per segment, not per business, and use it to find the stage causing the most drop-off or churn.

What the template covers. Five stages, four rows.

A customer experience journey map differs from a simpler buyer journey map in one important way: it does not stop at the sale. It runs across five stages, awareness, consideration, decision, onboarding, and ongoing use and renewal, tracking the full relationship rather than just the path to a signed deal. That matters because most of the customer experience that actually determines retention and referrals happens after the contract is signed, not before it.

Against those five stages, the template tracks four consistent rows: customer actions (what they are actually doing at this stage, requesting a demo, inviting colleagues to a trial, raising a support ticket), touchpoints (every place they interact with the business, website, sales call, onboarding email, support chat), thoughts and emotions (their honest internal state, not a marketing paraphrase of it), and pain points and opportunities (where the experience currently breaks down, and what would fix it).

The onboarding-to-success handoff is the row that breaks most often in practice. A customer who has already explained their goals, team structure and specific use case to a sales rep during the decision stage frequently has to repeat all of it to a different person during onboarding, because the two functions do not share notes properly. That repeated explanation is a small thing on its own, but it is one of the clearest, most fixable signs of a business that has not actually mapped its customer experience, only its sales process.

How to use it. Evidence first, one segment at a time.

Build the map from real sources, sales call recordings, onboarding call notes, support tickets, churn interviews and product usage data, rather than a team's collective guess about what customers experience. Gartner's research on customer journey mapping has found that a large majority of customer experience professionals report their organisation has struggled to actually use its journey maps to improve the experience, and that only around a third of teams building end-to-end maps have three or more years of practice doing it. Thin evidence behind the map, rather than the map format itself, is usually the reason it fails to change anything.

Map one customer segment at a time. A business selling both a low-touch self-serve product and a high-touch enterprise deal needs two separate maps, because the touchpoints, pain points and even the emotional tone at each stage differ meaningfully between the two. Trying to force both into one map produces something too generic to act on for either segment.

Once a stage's pain point is identified, assign it a specific, named owner and a fix with a deadline, not a general note that "onboarding needs work." A map that ends in a list of vague observations rarely survives past the workshop that produced it; a map that ends in three owned, dated fixes usually does.

Keep the finished map to a single page per segment, printed or projected large enough that a room can point at a specific cell during the workshop rather than scrolling through a long document. The discipline of forcing every observation into one of the four rows is what makes the exercise genuinely useful: disagreement over where a piece of evidence belongs, in "pain point" versus "touchpoint," for instance, is often where the most useful conversation in the room actually happens, not a distraction from the real work.

A worked example. A hospitality tech SaaS renewal.

A hospitality technology company selling booking software to independent hotels noticed a pattern: customers who churned at renewal had almost always raised the same complaint months earlier, that nobody from the company had checked in with them since onboarding finished. The team built a customer experience journey map for their core segment, an independent hotel general manager, using twelve recent churn interviews and a review of support ticket history.

The map showed the pain clearly in the "ongoing use and renewal" stage: the only scheduled touchpoint after onboarding was the renewal call itself, three months before contract expiry. By then, a GM whose usage had already been quietly declining for two months had mentally moved on, and the renewal call arrived too late to change anything. The "onboarding" stage, by contrast, scored well: customers consistently described it as smooth and well supported.

The fix the map surfaced was specific: a usage-triggered check-in at the 90-day mark, before the renewal conversation, flagged automatically when a hotel's login frequency dropped below a set threshold. That single change, sourced directly from what the map exposed rather than a general "improve customer success" initiative, gave the success team a genuine early warning instead of finding out about a disengaged customer at the same moment as the renewal deadline.

A customer experience journey map built this way, from evidence rather than assumption, tends to sit well alongside a narrower buyer journey map covering the path to purchase, and often benefits from starting with an empathy map for the segment first, since the emotional detail from that exercise is what makes the "thoughts and emotions" row specific rather than generic. Whichever order the exercises run in, the same rule holds: evidence in, one segment at a time, and a named, dated fix out the other end.

The template is deliberately reusable rather than a one-off exercise. Once a business has built its first map properly, refreshing it for a second segment, or updating it after a product change, takes a fraction of the time the first pass did, because the evidence-gathering habits, pulling call recordings, reviewing support tickets, checking usage data, are already in place. That is usually the point at which a customer experience journey map stops being a workshop artefact and becomes a genuine, living reference the team returns to whenever retention or referral numbers move and nobody is quite sure why.

Common questions.

What is the difference between a customer experience journey map and a buyer journey map?

A buyer journey map usually stops at the point of purchase, covering awareness through to the decision to buy. A customer experience journey map continues past that point, through onboarding, ongoing use, renewal and advocacy, since the experience that drives retention and referrals happens after the sale, not before it.

How many stages should a customer experience journey map have?

Five is a practical default for most B2B businesses: awareness, consideration, decision, onboarding, and ongoing use and renewal. Fewer stages risk missing a genuine shift in customer behaviour; more than six or seven tends to fragment the map to the point where patterns become harder to spot rather than easier.

What is the biggest mistake teams make when using this template?

Filling it in from internal assumption rather than real evidence, support tickets, sales call notes, churn interviews and usage data, and building one generic map for every customer type rather than a separate map for each meaningfully different segment. Gartner's research on customer journey mapping has found that a large majority of customer experience professionals say their organisation has struggled to turn a completed map into actual CX improvement, and thin evidence behind the map is a common cause.

How often should a customer experience journey map be updated?

At minimum, once a year, or immediately after a significant product change, pricing change or a new competitor entering the market that shifts how customers evaluate or use the product. A map built from data that is two or three years stale usually still looks tidy on a slide and is quietly wrong in practice.

Who should be in the room when building the map?

A cross-functional group, not one department: sales or marketing for the early stages, customer success or support for onboarding and ongoing use, and product for the underlying experience itself. Each function typically holds evidence the others do not see.
