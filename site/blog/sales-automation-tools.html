CRM automation & workflow

The best sales automation tools. And how to choose between them.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.5 July 20266 min read

Sales automation tools fall into three types: automation built into your CRM (Salesforce Flow, HubSpot workflows), dedicated sales engagement platforms (Outreach, Salesloft, Apollo) built for structured outbound, and no-code connectors (Zapier, Make) that link the two. Most founder-led teams should exhaust the first before paying for the second.

The short answer. Match the tool to the job, not the other way round.

Sales automation tools split into three practical categories, and the mistake I see most often is founders shopping in the second category before they have exhausted the first. The first is automation that lives inside the CRM you already pay for: Salesforce Flow, HubSpot workflows and sequences, Pipedrive automations. The second is a dedicated sales engagement platform, built specifically for high-volume, multi-channel outbound: Outreach, Salesloft, Apollo. The third is a connector layer, Zapier or Make, that sits between your CRM and everything else and moves data where native automation can't reach.

The sales force automation software market itself is not small or slowing down. Mordor Intelligence values it at US$11.64 billion in 2025, forecasting growth to US$27.3 billion by 2035, an 8.9% annual rate, with cloud deployments already accounting for 57.4% of that revenue and sales force automation holding close to 29% of the wider CRM market. That scale is exactly why the category is crowded with tools that overlap more than their marketing admits, and why the honest starting question is not "which tool" but "which layer of automation do I actually need yet".

Get that sequencing wrong and the usual result is a founder-led team paying for a sales engagement platform to run sequences a CRM workflow could have handled for free, while the actual bottleneck (a lead sitting unrouted for two days, a follow-up that only happens if someone remembers) never gets touched.

How they differ. Native automation, sales engagement platforms and connectors.

CRM-native automation is rules running against records you already have: when a deal moves to a stage, create a task; when a lead is created, assign it to the right rep by territory; when a form is submitted, send a templated email. It's included in most CRM tiers (sometimes gated behind a higher plan) and it needs no separate login for the team to check. Its ceiling is genuine multi-step, multi-channel sequencing: most native workflow builders start to strain once you're coordinating calls, emails and LinkedIn touches across several weeks for hundreds of prospects at once.

That's the gap sales engagement platforms are built to close. Outreach, Salesloft and Apollo exist specifically to sequence outbound across channels, track reply and engagement signals, and give reps a single queue of next actions rather than a CRM view cluttered with everything else. They're worth the extra subscription once a team is running structured outbound at real volume; they're an expensive way to send emails a CRM workflow could already send if the volume isn't there yet.

Connector tools sit apart from both. Zapier and Make don't generate outbound activity themselves, they move data between systems that don't talk to each other natively: pushing a new Stripe customer into the CRM, syncing a form submission into a spreadsheet the finance team still uses, alerting a Slack channel when a deal closes. For a founder-led team running four or five disconnected tools, a handful of well-built connector workflows often removes more manual re-entry, faster, than either of the other two categories.

Which to use and when. Team size and process maturity decide it, not the demo.

A small founder-led team with one or two people carrying quota should start and often finish with CRM-native automation. The volume of outbound rarely justifies a dedicated platform's licence cost, and the bigger win is usually mundane: consistent lead routing, a follow-up task nobody forgets, a re-engagement sequence that fires on a schedule instead of when someone has a spare afternoon. If your re-engagement flows are still running from memory or a spreadsheet rather than a scheduled workflow, that is nearly always the first thing worth fixing before any new platform gets a look in.

A dedicated sales engagement platform earns its cost once a team has a genuine SDR function running structured, multi-touch outbound across dozens or hundreds of prospects a week, and needs the reply tracking and cadence management that a CRM workflow builder was never designed to do well. Buying one earlier than that usually means paying for capability the team isn't yet using, and adding a second system that has to be kept in sync with the CRM by hand.

Connector tools earn their place the moment a team is manually copying data between two or more systems on any kind of regular schedule. That's rarely a "which tool" decision so much as a "what's the one recurring manual task costing the most hours this month" decision, then building a single workflow to remove it before moving to the next one.

Take a concrete example. A five-person founder-led sales team is currently sending manual follow-up emails after every demo, re-typing new leads from a contact form into the CRM by hand, and running a re-engagement sequence from a shared spreadsheet that gets updated whenever someone remembers. None of that justifies a sales engagement platform. A CRM workflow can send the demo follow-up automatically, route the form submission straight into the right owner's queue, and fire the re-engagement sequence on a schedule, at zero extra licence cost. The same team, once it hires two dedicated SDRs running 200 cold outbound touches a week across email, call and LinkedIn, has genuinely outgrown that native workflow builder and is the point where Outreach, Salesloft or Apollo starts to earn its cost.

How Lauren would decide. Audit the manual work before buying anything.

Before I recommend a platform to a client, I ask them to list every manual, repetitive step someone on the team does more than once a week: chasing a follow-up, re-typing a lead into a spreadsheet, manually assigning a new enquiry. Most of that list gets solved by automation already sitting unused inside the CRM they're paying for. That's usually the fastest, cheapest fix, and it's exactly how Lauren approaches CRM automation with clients who assume the answer is always a new subscription.

Only once that list is genuinely exhausted, and the bottleneck is clearly volume or multi-channel sequencing rather than missing automation, does a dedicated platform make sense. Buying the tool before doing that audit is how teams end up with three overlapping systems, none of them fully adopted, and a CRM that's messier for the automation rather than cleaner.

The audit itself doesn't need to be complicated. A single working session with whoever actually does the manual task, not just the manager who assumes they know the process, usually surfaces the real bottleneck within an hour. Ask what gets done manually every single week, how long it takes, and whether it's rule-based (always the same steps) or judgement-based (depends on the situation). Rule-based tasks are automation candidates immediately. Judgement-based ones are exactly what a rep's time should be protected for, not routed into a workflow that can't make the call. Map the manual work first, in that order. The tool choice gets a lot easier once you know exactly what it needs to replace.

Common questions.

What is the difference between CRM automation and a sales engagement platform?

CRM automation runs inside the system you already use to store customer data (Salesforce Flow, HubSpot workflows) and handles record updates, task creation and simple email sequences. A sales engagement platform such as Outreach, Salesloft or Apollo is a separate layer built specifically for structured, multi-step outbound: call, email and LinkedIn touches sequenced across weeks, with reply detection built in.

Do I need a dedicated sales engagement tool, or is my CRM's built-in automation enough?

For most founder-led teams, no. If your reps are not yet running high-volume, multi-channel outbound sequences, CRM-native automation covers reminders, lead routing and simple nurture emails without adding another subscription and another place for data to fall out of sync. Add a dedicated platform once outbound volume genuinely outgrows what workflows can sequence cleanly.

What does sales automation software typically cost?

CRM-native automation is usually bundled into the CRM licence tier you already pay for, sometimes gated behind a higher plan. Dedicated sales engagement platforms are a separate per-seat subscription on top of the CRM, and connector tools such as Zapier or Make are priced by task volume. Budget for the CRM tier first before adding a second platform.

Can sales automation replace a salesperson's judgement calls?

No, and treating it that way is where most automation projects go wrong. Automation should take over the repetitive, rule-based steps (send the follow-up, update the field, create the task) so a rep's time goes on the calls, objections and negotiations that genuinely need a person. If a workflow starts making judgement calls about deal quality, pull it back.

How do I stop my CRM data getting messy when I add more automation?

Fix the underlying field structure and required-field rules before switching automation on, not after. Automation reliably amplifies whatever state your data is already in: clean, well-defined fields get automated consistently, and messy or duplicated fields get automated messily, faster, and at greater scale.
