Salesforce vs Zoho CRM. Which one actually fits your team.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.15 July 20268 min read

Salesforce suits a team with the budget and complexity to justify deep customisation and a dedicated admin. Zoho CRM suits a founder-led team that wants strong functionality without the price tag or the implementation overhead. The right choice usually comes down to budget, team size and how much customisation the sales process genuinely needs.

What to weigh up. The real decision behind salesforce vs zoho.

Founders searching salesforce vs zoho are usually choosing between two very different companies, not two flavours of the same product. Salesforce is the market's incumbent: IDC's Worldwide Semiannual Software Tracker ranked it the number one CRM provider by revenue for the thirteenth consecutive year in its 2025 report, with a 20.0% share of the global CRM market. Zoho is the value challenger, a privately held, self-funded company that built its reputation on undercutting the majors on price while still shipping a genuinely capable CRM as part of its wider Zoho One suite of more than 45 business apps.

Neither fact settles which one is right for a specific team. What does is three questions: how much the sales process needs customising beyond a standard pipeline, how many other systems the CRM has to talk to, and what the business can commit to paying per seat once the team doubles. Salesforce answers the first two questions well and the third one expensively. Zoho answers the third question well and the first two adequately for most SMB sales motions, until a business grows past the point where "adequate" is good enough.

The other factor that gets missed in a straight features comparison is implementation time. Zoho's simpler data model and largely self-serve setup mean a small team can be live within days. Salesforce's flexibility is also its complexity: the same power that lets an enterprise build a bespoke approval chain across five departments means a badly scoped CRM implementation can drag on for months and still not fit how the team actually sells.

Salesforce raised list prices around 6% across its Enterprise and Unlimited tiers in August 2025, which is worth factoring into a multi-year budget rather than just the first year's quote. Zoho's pricing has stayed comparatively flat, and its annual billing discount against monthly (roughly 20 to 40% lower per seat) tends to be steeper too, so the true gap at scale is usually wider than the headline numbers above suggest.

Support and ecosystem depth are where the price gap partly explains itself. Salesforce's AppExchange runs into the thousands of listed integrations and its partner network of certified implementation consultants is genuinely global, which matters for a business with complex, region-specific requirements. Zoho's Marketplace is smaller but covers the integrations most SMB stacks actually need, and its support model leans more heavily on self-serve documentation and community forums than on a dedicated account team, which is a reasonable trade for a business not yet large enough to need one.

Which to choose and when. The actual triggers.

Choose Salesforce when sales is one of several departments that need to sit on the same platform, marketing, service and sales sharing one customer record, when the approval chains and territory rules are genuinely complex, or when the business already runs on an enterprise stack (NetSuite, SAP, a large marketing cloud) that a Salesforce integration handles more cleanly than a mid-market CRM would. It is also the safer long-term bet for a team that expects to be several hundred seats within a few years, since re-platforming later is expensive.

Choose Zoho when the business is bootstrapped or budget-conscious, when the sales process is genuinely straightforward, prospect, qualify, propose, close, without a need for elaborate multi-team workflows, or when bundling the CRM with the rest of the operating stack (support desk, finance, marketing) inside Zoho One is worth more than having the single deepest CRM on the market. A ten-person team choosing between the two on price alone will usually land on Zoho and be perfectly well served by it.

The one scenario worth flagging separately is a team that has outgrown a spreadsheet but has not yet outgrown simplicity. That team should resist the pull towards Salesforce's brand recognition and pick the platform that matches today's actual complexity, not the complexity the business hopes to have in three years.

How Lauren would decide. The practical test.

I ask clients to list, honestly, which of these three things are true right now: sales needs to share a live customer record with marketing or support on the same platform; the pipeline has approval steps that genuinely differ by deal size, region or product; and the business has, or is about to hire, someone whose job includes CRM administration. Two or three yeses point towards Salesforce. Zero or one points towards Zoho, and towards spending the price difference on something that moves revenue faster than a platform upgrade would.

Working across GCC-based teams, I have implemented both, and the pattern holds regardless of geography: the CRM that gets used is the one configured around how the team actually sells, not the one with the longer feature list. Getting the selection and implementation right the first time matters more than which logo is on the login page, because a well-configured Zoho instance a team actually uses beats a half-finished Salesforce rollout every time.

Whichever platform you land on, run the same due diligence you would apply to any six-figure, multi-year commitment: price the tier you will actually need at scale, not the entry tier used to win the trial, and get a written implementation timeline before signing, not after.

Common questions.

Is Zoho CRM as powerful as Salesforce?

For most founder-led sales processes, yes. Zoho covers pipeline management, automation, reporting and integrations well enough that most SMB teams never hit its ceiling. Salesforce's extra power shows up in deep customisation, cross-department platforms and enterprise-scale governance, which most teams under fifty seats do not yet need.

Which is cheaper, Salesforce or Zoho?

Zoho, at every tier. Zoho's entry Standard plan runs around $14 per user per month against Salesforce's Starter Suite at $25, and the gap widens further up the range: Zoho's Enterprise plan sits near $40 per user per month against Salesforce Enterprise at $175 (2026 public pricing).

Can I switch from Zoho to Salesforce later if I outgrow it?

Yes, and it is a common path. Migrating custom fields, workflow rules and historical records takes proper planning rather than a straight export and import, so treat it as a project with a data-cleaning phase, not a weekend task, and budget implementation time separately from the licence cost.

Does Zoho CRM integrate with other business tools?

Yes, both natively through the wider Zoho One suite of over 45 apps, and through third-party connectors and its own API. It is not as deep a marketplace as Salesforce's AppExchange, but for most SMB stacks, accounting, email, support, marketing, the coverage is more than adequate.

Do I need a consultant to set up Salesforce or Zoho?

Zoho is realistically self-serve for a small team willing to spend a few days configuring it. Salesforce's flexibility is also its complexity: most teams get a cleaner result, and avoid rebuilding a badly configured instance six months later, by bringing in someone who has implemented it before.
