Fractional sales leadership

When to hire a fractional CRO. Five signals worth acting on.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.8 July 20268 min read

Hire a fractional CRO when revenue has stalled despite steady leads, the founder is still running sales personally past roughly $2 million in revenue, or the business needs credible revenue leadership for a raise or exit before it can justify a full-time, $250,000-plus hire. Most engagements run 10 to 20 hours a week for six to twelve months.

The short answer. Five signals, not a headcount rule.

There is no revenue threshold that automatically means it's time to hire a fractional CRO. What matters is whether specific, recognisable problems have shown up: growth that has flattened despite a healthy pipeline, a founder still closing every deal personally, a sales team with no shared forecasting process, or an investor conversation that needs a credible commercial leader in the room. A fractional CRO fixes a function, not a headcount gap, so the decision should start with the problem, not the org chart.

The cost case is straightforward once the numbers are next to each other. Fractional CRO retainers typically run $8,000 to $17,000 a month for 25 to 40 hours, against a true employer cost of $270,000 to $350,000 a year for a full-time CRO once salary, benefits, payroll tax and equity are counted, according to Fractionus's 2026 fractional executive benchmarks. That gap is why the fractional model is the obvious first move for most businesses under roughly $30 million in revenue: it buys senior revenue leadership at 60 to 75 per cent less cash cost, without the equity dilution a full-time executive hire usually carries at that stage.

What a fractional CRO actually does is closer to a full-time chief revenue officer than a consultant. They own the revenue number, run the pipeline reviews, coach or manage the sales team directly, and report against a forecast they built, not one they were handed. The "fractional" part describes the hours, not the level of accountability.

It also helps to be clear about what a fractional CRO is not. It is not a part-time salesperson brought in to close a handful of deals, and it is not the same engagement as a growth consultant who advises from the outside. The distinguishing feature is ownership: a fractional CRO carries the revenue target as their own, the same way a full-time hire would, and is judged against it at the end of the engagement.

How it works in practice. The signals that mean it's time.

1. Growth has stalled despite steady lead volume. If marketing is delivering roughly the same number of qualified leads month over month but revenue has flattened or slipped, the problem usually sits in how those leads are being converted, not how many arrive. That is a sales process and pipeline discipline problem, which is exactly what a fractional CRO is brought in to diagnose and fix.

2. The founder is still running sales personally past $2 to $3 million in revenue. Founder-led sales works well early, then becomes the ceiling. Past that point, the founder's time is the single most constrained resource in the business, and every hour spent chasing a deal personally is an hour not spent on product, hiring or the next stage of growth. A fractional CRO takes over the sales motion the founder built, without the six-figure commitment a full-time hire represents before the business has proven it needs one permanently.

3. There is no shared forecasting or pipeline process. If revenue numbers depend on one person's gut feel, or the pipeline report a founder relies on is known internally to be unreliable, that is a structural gap a fractional CRO closes early, usually within the first month, by installing defined pipeline stages, a forecasting cadence and a single source of truth the whole team trusts.

4. The business needs a go-to-market strategy before it can justify a full-time hire. Businesses entering a new segment, launching a new product line, or expanding into a new market often need senior strategic input for a defined period rather than a permanent leader from day one. A fractional CRO can build the go-to-market plan, test it, and either hand it to an existing team or help recruit the person who runs it long term.

5. A fundraise or exit needs credible revenue leadership. Investors and acquirers weight revenue predictability heavily. A business raising a round or preparing for a sale with no named commercial leader, an unreliable forecast, or a founder who is also the head of sales, sends a specific kind of risk signal. A fractional CRO brought in ahead of that process gives the numbers a named owner and a defensible forecast methodology before the questions get asked in diligence.

Not every business showing one of these signals needs a fractional CRO immediately. A business with only signal one, flat growth despite steady leads, might just need a sharper conversion process, which a shorter, narrower engagement can fix. It is when two or more signals show up together, a stalled forecast alongside a founder still closing deals personally, for instance, that a broader fractional CRO engagement tends to pay for itself fastest.

What good looks like. A scoped engagement with a clear exit point.

A well-run fractional CRO engagement has a defined scope, not an open-ended retainer. Most run 10 to 20 hours a week, sometimes structured as two or three fixed days, with named deliverables in the first 90 days: a documented pipeline and forecasting process, a clear view of what is and is not working in the current sales motion, and a small number of measurable targets rather than a vague mandate to "grow revenue".

Engagement length matters more than most businesses plan for upfront. Six to twelve months is typical for a first engagement, long enough to install process and prove it holds under real pipeline pressure, then either extend, convert the relationship to full-time, or hand over cleanly to a permanent hire the fractional CRO has helped recruit and onboard. The businesses that get the most value treat this handover as part of the original scope, not an afterthought decided once the engagement is already ending. Our work on fractional sales leadership and the fractional CRO service both start from that same scoped-engagement structure, because an open-ended brief is where fractional relationships tend to drift.

Good fractional CRO work also looks like specificity in how success is measured. Rather than a general revenue target, the strongest engagements track leading indicators, pipeline coverage ratio, average deal cycle length, forecast accuracy against actuals, alongside the lagging revenue number, so both the founder and the fractional CRO can see whether the fix is working before a full quarter has passed. Our guide to fractional revenue leadership covers how that reporting cadence should be structured month to month, and the different fractional sales models post breaks down the retainer, project and hybrid structures a fractional CRO relationship can take depending on what the business actually needs.

A useful test before signing anything: ask the prospective fractional CRO to describe their first 30 days in specific terms, which pipeline data they will pull, who they will interview on the team, what the first report to the founder will contain. A vague answer here, "I'll get to know the business," usually predicts a vague engagement later. A specific answer is a reasonable proxy for how the rest of the engagement will run.

Pitfalls to avoid. Where fractional CRO hires go wrong.

Hiring too early, before there is a real sales motion to fix. A fractional CRO improves and scales an existing process; a business with no leads, no product-market fit signal and no sales history yet needs a different kind of help first, usually a founder still selling personally to learn what actually works.

Treating the role as a part-time salesperson. A fractional CRO who spends their hours closing individual deals rather than building the process, coaching the team and fixing the forecast has been under-scoped. The value is in the system they leave behind, not the deals they personally close during the engagement.

No handover plan from day one. Engagements that end with no agreed next step, extend, convert, or recruit a permanent replacement, tend to leave a gap exactly when the fix is starting to show results. Agree the exit path before the engagement starts, not six weeks before it ends.

Withholding real authority. A fractional CRO asked to advise but not to actually run pipeline reviews, set targets or manage the sales team rarely produces the result a full engagement would. If the role is scoped as advisory only, it should be priced and framed as consulting, not as fractional leadership.

Skipping the diagnostic phase to save time. The first two to three weeks of a good engagement are spent understanding what is actually happening in the pipeline before changing anything. Fractional CROs who arrive with a template playbook and start implementing on day one, without first checking whether it fits this business's actual sales motion, tend to produce changes that do not stick.

Comparing candidates purely on day rate. A cheaper fractional CRO who has never worked in your sector or at your business's size can end up costing more in lost time than a pricier one who has solved this exact problem before and needs far fewer weeks to show results. Weigh the day rate against relevant track record, not against the number alone.

Common questions.

What does a fractional CRO cost compared to a full-time hire?

Fractional CRO retainers typically run from $8,000 to $17,000 a month for 25 to 40 hours, against a true employer cost of $270,000 to $350,000 a year for a full-time CRO once salary, benefits and equity are counted, according to Fractionus's 2026 fractional executive benchmarks. Most founder-led businesses save 60 to 75 per cent of cash by going fractional first.

How many hours a week does a fractional CRO typically work?

Most engagements run 10 to 20 hours a week, sometimes structured as two or three fixed days, rather than full-time hours split across clients. The hours matter less than the outcomes attached to them: a clear scope with named deliverables beats an open-ended day rate.

What is the difference between a fractional CRO and a sales consultant?

A sales consultant advises and hands you a plan. A fractional CRO sits inside the business, owns the revenue number, runs the pipeline reviews and manages the sales team directly, with the same accountability a full-time hire would carry, just for part of the week.

How long does a typical fractional CRO engagement last?

Six to twelve months is typical for a first engagement, long enough to fix pipeline structure, forecasting and process, then either extend, convert to full-time, or hand over to a hire the fractional CRO helped recruit and onboard.

Can a fractional CRO become a full-time hire later?

Sometimes, but it should never be the default plan. A cleaner path is to agree upfront that the fractional CRO builds the function and helps recruit its permanent leader, so the business is not left searching for a full-time replacement from a standing start.

What revenue stage typically needs a fractional CRO?

Businesses roughly between $1 million and $15 million in annual revenue are the most common fit: too large for the founder to keep running sales alone, not yet at the size or funding stage that justifies a $250,000-plus full-time executive hire.
