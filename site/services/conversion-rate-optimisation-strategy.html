Conversion & funnel optimisation

Conversion rate optimisation strategy: systematic improvement, not guesswork.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.22 June 20267 min read

A conversion rate optimisation strategy is the structured process of using data, user research and systematic testing to improve the percentage of visitors who take a target action. Lauren Pearson's CRO strategy service builds this framework for your specific funnel: what to test, in what order, and how to measure whether it is working.

Most conversion rate optimisation fails not because the changes are wrong but because the process is wrong. Businesses run tests based on opinion, implement changes without a control, or fix one element of a page and declare victory without checking whether the improvement held. The result is a conversion rate that moves up and down without anyone understanding why. A conversion rate optimisation strategy is what changes that.

The difference between businesses with consistently improving conversion rates and those that plateau is almost always the same: one group has a structured programme with a clear method for deciding what to test, how to test it, and what to do with the results. The other makes changes based on whoever last read a CRO article. The good news is that building the structured programme is not complicated. It requires the right framework, the right data, and someone who can hold the process together across a period of weeks and months.

This service sits within the broader conversion rate optimisation practice. It is designed for businesses that want to build a proper CRO programme, not just run a few tests and hope for the best.

Who this is for.

A conversion rate optimisation strategy engagement is the right choice in several situations:

  • You have been making conversion changes, either in-house or with an agency, but the results have been inconsistent. Some changes helped, some did not, and you do not have a clear view of why. You want a structured approach that builds understanding rather than producing random results.
  • You are starting a CRO programme from scratch and want to build it correctly from the beginning rather than developing bad habits that you have to undo later. Getting the methodology right in the first few months saves a significant amount of wasted testing effort.
  • Your current CRO activity is focused on easy-to-change elements, such as button colours and minor copy tweaks, rather than the structural issues that would actually move the needle. You want a prioritisation framework that directs effort toward what matters commercially.
  • You have grown through paid acquisition and want to improve the efficiency of that spend. A CRO strategy that systematically improves conversion rates across your paid landing pages reduces cost per acquisition as traffic scales.
  • You are planning a website rebuild or redesign and want a tested brief: evidence about what is working, what is not, and what the new site needs to do differently, rather than building based on aesthetic preference.
  • Your CRO work is disconnected from your commercial goals. Tests are run and won but the business does not feel it in revenue, because the things being optimised are not the things that drive growth.

What is included.

A conversion rate optimisation strategy engagement has four components, each building on the last.

  • Research and diagnostic phase. Before any testing begins, the engagement starts with a thorough understanding of the current state: analytics review to identify where the funnel is losing visitors, session recording and heatmap analysis to understand what visitors are actually doing, and a structured review of any existing test data to determine what has been learned so far. The research phase answers the question "what is causing the current conversion rate?" before asking "what should we test?"
  • Hypothesis development. Each test in a CRO programme should be built around a specific, falsifiable hypothesis: "changing X will improve Y because we believe Z is the reason for the current performance." Hypothesis development is the translation of the research findings into testable propositions, ordered by expected impact and the effort required to run the test. The frameworks used, typically PIE (Potential, Importance, Ease) or ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease), provide a consistent basis for prioritisation rather than relying on opinion.
  • Testing programme design. A structured testing plan covering which hypotheses to test first, what the test design looks like (what is being changed, what the control is, what the success metric is), the sample size and duration required to reach statistical significance, and the governance process for calling results and moving to the next test. This design is built for your specific traffic volumes, conversion goals and technical environment.
  • Measurement and learning framework. A CRO strategy only compounds if learnings are documented and used. The measurement framework sets out how test results are recorded, how insights are shared across the team, how winning variants are implemented and monitored over time, and how the overall conversion rate is tracked against the baseline. This prevents the common failure mode where tests are run, results noted, and the learning is immediately lost when attention moves to the next project.

How the engagement works.

The strategy engagement runs in two distinct phases: building the strategy, and then running it.

Phase one: strategy build (weeks one to four). Research, analysis and hypothesis development. The output at the end of this phase is a CRO strategy document covering: the current state of conversion performance across the funnel, the highest-impact opportunities ranked by expected commercial value, a prioritised testing backlog with specific hypotheses, the measurement framework, and a governance process for managing the programme. This document is the operating brief for everything that follows.

Phase two: programme management (ongoing from week four). Running the testing programme against the strategy: executing tests, reviewing results, updating the backlog, and iterating the strategy as new evidence comes in. The programme is reviewed fortnightly, with a monthly summary of what has been tested, what the results show, and what the next quarter of tests looks like. The strategy is a living document that evolves as the programme generates data.

The strategy engagement can run as a standalone deliverable, where the output is a strategy document and testing brief handed to an in-house team or agency to execute. It can also run as an end-to-end programme where strategy and execution are managed together. Outcomes and proof.

The primary output of a CRO strategy engagement is a programme that produces consistent, compounding improvement rather than one-off wins that are not sustained. What that looks like in practice:

  • A clear picture of where the funnel is losing conversions and why, rather than an aggregate conversion rate that does not explain anything and cannot be acted on.
  • A prioritised backlog of tests ordered by expected commercial impact, so that the limited time available for CRO work is spent on the things that matter most rather than the things that are easiest to change.
  • A testing programme where each test is built on a specific hypothesis, run to statistical significance, and documented so that the learning is usable for future tests and future site builds.
  • A conversion rate that improves over time and is tracked against a defined baseline, so the commercial impact of the programme is measurable rather than anecdotal.
  • An internal or cross-agency team that understands the methodology and can continue running the programme as the business evolves, rather than depending on external input for every test.

Businesses that invest in the strategy layer before scaling their CRO execution consistently get more out of the same testing effort. Knowing what to test, and in what order, is often worth more than having the capacity to run many tests quickly if those tests are not asking the right questions. A well-built conversion rate optimisation strategy is the difference between a programme that learns and one that spins.

Common questions.

What is a conversion rate optimisation strategy?

A conversion rate optimisation strategy is the structured process of using data, user research and systematic testing to improve the percentage of visitors who take a target action. It covers three things: understanding why current conversion rates are what they are, deciding which changes are most worth testing and in what order, and measuring whether the changes produce reliable improvement. Without a strategy, CRO becomes a series of one-off changes based on opinion, which produces inconsistent and often contradictory results.

What is the difference between a CRO strategy and just running A/B tests?

A/B tests are a tool; a CRO strategy is the framework that tells you what to test, in what order, and what to do with the results. Without a strategy, most businesses test whatever is easy to change, rather than what is most likely to produce meaningful improvement. A CRO strategy starts with research, identifies the highest-impact opportunities, structures tests to answer specific hypotheses, and builds a learning programme where each test informs the next.

How do you prioritise what to test in a CRO strategy?

The most widely used prioritisation frameworks in CRO are PIE (Potential, Importance, Ease) and ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease). Both score potential changes on multiple dimensions to identify which tests are most likely to produce meaningful results with the resources available. In practice, the best prioritisation combines a scoring framework with qualitative judgement about which parts of the funnel, if improved, would have the most downstream commercial impact.

How much traffic do I need to run a CRO strategy?

You need enough traffic to reach statistical significance within a reasonable time frame, typically four to eight weeks per test. As a rough guide, pages with fewer than 500 monthly conversions are harder to test rigorously because tests take too long to call. Below that threshold, a CRO strategy can still be useful, but the focus shifts toward research, quick wins and page-level improvements rather than formal A/B testing, until traffic volumes grow.

Should a CRO strategy be handled in-house or with an external specialist?

Either can work, but the common failure mode in-house is that CRO gets deprioritised in favour of campaigns and launches, tests run without clear hypotheses, and learnings are not documented so the programme does not compound. An external specialist brings a structured methodology, an outside perspective on where the real friction is, and accountability for running a programme rather than one-off tests. Many businesses use an external strategy and brief layer with in-house or agency execution.

How does a CRO strategy connect to the rest of our marketing?

A CRO strategy should be built against your traffic sources, offer and conversion goal, not designed in isolation. Changes that improve conversion on organic traffic may not improve it on paid traffic, because the audiences are different. A good CRO strategy segments by traffic source and audience, identifies the specific friction for each segment, and connects the conversion rate data back to revenue metrics so that the impact of improvements is measured in commercial terms, not just percentage uplifts.
