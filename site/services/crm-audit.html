CRM implementation & selection

A CRM audit that shows you what's working and what to fix.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.18 June 20267 min read

A CRM audit is a structured review of how your CRM is configured, used and governed. It surfaces data quality issues, broken workflows, adoption gaps and reporting blind spots, then produces a ranked action list so your team knows what to fix, what to retire and what to build on. Lauren Pearson runs CRM audits for B2B teams in the UK and globally.

Most CRMs deteriorate quietly. The sales team was trained at go-live, the pipeline looked right for the first few months, and then something shifted. Data got stale. A workflow stopped firing correctly. The team started keeping a spreadsheet on the side because the CRM could not be trusted for the morning call. The pipeline report became something the founder and sales lead argued about rather than agreed on.

A CRM audit does not assume the system is broken. It maps exactly what is happening: which parts of the configuration are sound, which are causing problems, and which should simply be retired. The output is a ranked action list. Not a proposal to replace everything, but a clear picture of where you stand and what to do next. Who benefits from a CRM audit.

A CRM audit is most useful in three situations.

The first is when the system is live but results have stalled. The CRM was set up correctly 12 to 18 months ago, the team is using it, but the pipeline data is inconsistent, forecasts are unreliable, and leadership has stopped trusting what the report shows. The gap between the CRM and reality is widening, but it is not clear where the breakdown started.

The second is when the team is about to grow. Bringing new sales hires into a poorly configured CRM passes the problem forward rather than solving it. An audit before onboarding new reps means the system they inherit is one that actually works, with clean data, clear stage definitions and automations that fire correctly.

The third is before a migration or re-implementation. If you are moving to a new CRM or upgrading your current platform, auditing what you have first means you carry only the clean data, the working workflows and the useful history into the new environment. Bad data and broken workflows follow you from one system to the next unless you audit before you move.

Businesses already working with Lauren on CRM implementation or CRM consulting sometimes commission an audit when a significant process change happens: a new product line, a market expansion or a change in the sales team's structure. The process changes, the CRM has not been updated to reflect it, and the gaps need mapping before they cause bigger problems.

What a CRM audit covers.

The audit reviews six areas. Each one gets a written finding and a recommended action.

Data quality. The foundation of a reliable CRM is data you can trust. The audit looks at contact and deal record completeness: what percentage of records have the key fields filled in correctly, how many duplicates exist, and what proportion of the data is stale or simply wrong. According to Validity's 2022 State of CRM Data Health and Opportunity report, poor data quality costs the average organisation roughly $12.9 million per year across sales, marketing and customer success. The audit shows where the gaps are and what it takes to close them.

Pipeline integrity. Stage definitions, exit criteria, deal age and deal velocity. A well-configured pipeline has stages that reflect how the team actually sells, with clear criteria for moving a deal forward rather than leaving it to each rep's interpretation. The audit identifies stages where deals are stalling, stages that everyone skips in practice, and gaps between what the pipeline structure says should happen and what the activity data shows actually does.

User adoption. The CRM is only as good as what gets recorded in it. The audit maps which team members are active, which are bypassing the system, and what kinds of activity are being logged versus what is not. If deals are being won without ever appearing in the pipeline, or if calls and emails are not being tracked, the data cannot represent the real commercial position. This section tells you the honest state of adoption, not the theoretical one.

Workflows and automations. Over time, automations drift. Triggers fire at the wrong point, sequences continue for contacts that should have exited, and automations built for a previous version of the sales process keep running on the new one. The audit checks whether the current automations are doing what they were designed to do, and which ones should be updated, paused or removed.

Reporting and dashboards. The audit looks at which dashboards are being used and which have been built but quietly ignored. It checks whether the metrics leadership relies on are pulling from the right data, and whether the report shared in the weekly call is actually answering the questions it is supposed to answer. A reliable forecast depends on accurate pipeline data. If the data feeding the forecast is wrong, the forecast is guesswork.

Integrations. Broken integrations are a common source of missing data that teams attribute to the wrong cause. The audit checks whether connected tools (email, marketing platform, billing system) are syncing correctly and whether the data flowing between systems is consistent and current.

How the engagement works.

The audit runs over two to three weeks.

The first week is access and data collection. Lauren gets read access to your CRM, pulls a sample of contact, deal and activity records, and maps the current pipeline structure, field usage, workflow configuration and integration status. No data is taken outside the platform; the audit is read-only.

The second week is analysis. The six areas are reviewed against what a functioning CRM should look like for a business at your size and with your sales process. Findings are documented with the specific records, fields or workflows that illustrate each issue, so the action list is precise rather than general.

At the end of the engagement you receive a written report with a prioritised action list. Each finding is categorised: fix now (the system cannot give you reliable data in its current state), fix next (meaningful impact but lower urgency), or consider removing (configuration that adds noise without value). The report is followed by a 60-minute call to walk through the findings and agree next steps.

The audit does not assume you need a full re-implementation. Many findings can be resolved internally in an afternoon. Others are larger projects. The report separates those clearly so you can decide what to act on yourselves and what would benefit from outside help. For teams that want to act on the findings with support, a follow-on CRM implementation engagement can be scoped directly from the audit report.

Outcomes and what to expect.

The concrete deliverable is a written report and a prioritised action list. Not a high-level observation, but a field-by-field, workflow-by-workflow review with specific recommended actions.

Most teams find two or three issues they did not know were problems. A frequent one is an automation built for a previous version of the sales process that has been quietly misfiring ever since. Another is a pipeline stage that the team skips in practice, which renders that part of the stage data meaningless for forecasting. A third is a field that leadership relies on for reporting but that only 40% of records actually have filled in.

The audit also gives you a baseline. If you are planning a CRM migration, a new product launch or a change to the sales team's structure, the audit is the starting point for understanding what you are working with. Decisions about a re-implementation are always better when they are made from a clear picture of the existing system rather than an assumed one.

Common questions.

What does a CRM audit actually cover?

A CRM audit reviews six areas: data quality, pipeline integrity, user adoption, workflow and automation health, reporting accuracy, and integration status. Each area gets a written finding and a specific recommended action. The output is a ranked action list, not a general observation. Every finding ties to actual records, workflows or configuration settings in your CRM.

How long does a CRM audit take?

The audit runs over two to three weeks. The first week covers access and data review. The second is analysis and report writing. You then receive the written report followed by a 60-minute walkthrough call. For a business with a single CRM and a straightforward sales process, two weeks is usually enough.

How much does a CRM audit cost?

The audit is a fixed-scope engagement with a fee agreed upfront. It is substantially less than a full implementation project because it is read-only and produces a report rather than a rebuilt system. Get in touch and we can discuss the scope and confirm the cost on a first call.

My team uses the CRM every day. Why do we still need an audit?

Daily usage does not prevent configuration drift. Workflows built for a previous sales process keep running after the process changes. Field coverage deteriorates as team habits shift. Automations break without warning. An audit is not about whether the team is using the CRM; it is about whether the CRM is doing what it should.

What happens after the audit? Do you fix the problems?

The audit itself is a discovery engagement. The report tells you what needs fixing and in what order. For teams that want help implementing the findings, that is a separate engagement scoped directly from the report. Lauren can run the fixes as a follow-on project, or the internal team can act on them independently using the report.

Which CRM platforms do you audit?

The most common platforms are HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive and Zoho CRM. If you are on a different platform, get in touch. The audit approach is the same regardless of the tool, and the findings will tell you whether the limitations are in the configuration or in the platform itself.
