CRM implementation & selection

A CRM audit that tells you what's broken and what to do next.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.17 June 20267 min read

A CRM audit is a structured review of your existing CRM: data quality, pipeline accuracy, process alignment and system configuration. Lauren Pearson runs CRM audits for UK founder-led teams to identify what is stopping the system from working and to provide a clear, prioritised fix list.

Most founder-led teams reach a point where the CRM does not reflect what is actually happening in the business. Deals live in the pipeline long past the point where they were lost. Activity is logged inconsistently, or not at all. The data behind the dashboard is not trusted by the team and is not trusted by leadership either. The forecast is a number on a screen that nobody believes.

A CRM audit identifies exactly what is wrong. Not by replacing the system or starting over, but by reviewing what is there: the data, the pipeline structure, how the team actually uses the CRM, whether the configuration matches the real sales process, and whether the integrations are working. You get a written report and a prioritised list of fixes, not a sales pitch for a new platform.

The audit sits within the wider CRM implementation and selection work I do, but it is a standalone engagement for teams that already have a CRM in place. If you are starting from scratch or the audit concludes a rebuild is warranted, that conversation happens separately.

Who a CRM audit is for.

The typical starting point is a CRM that has been running for a year or more and is causing friction rather than reducing it. The signs vary, but a few show up in almost every engagement.

Pipeline data that leadership does not trust. The numbers on the dashboard and the numbers the sales team gives verbally in the forecast meeting are different. Nobody is sure which one is closer to right. Decisions get made from instinct rather than data, not because the team is careless, but because the system does not give them a reason to trust it.

A high proportion of deals stuck in one stage. Stage two of a five-stage pipeline holds 60% of all open opportunities. Nobody can say why, or what it would take to move them. Some are live, some are dead, and the pipeline has become a repository rather than a working tool.

Inconsistent activity logging. Some reps record every call. Others record nothing. The contact timeline for half the database is blank, so when someone picks up an account they have never worked before, there is no history to read.

A growing suspicion that the wrong CRM is in use. Before committing to a migration, it is worth finding out whether the problem is the platform or the way it has been set up. In most cases it is the latter. An audit confirms which before any money changes hands on a new system.

The audit is also the right step before a senior hire. Bringing a sales manager or VP of sales into a CRM that does not work gives them a difficult first impression and makes their job harder from day one. Running the audit beforehand means they step into a system they can trust.

What the audit covers.

The engagement reviews five areas of the CRM.

Data quality

A review of contact and company records: duplicate entries, missing required fields, contacts with no owner, deals linked to the wrong company, and records that have not been updated in six months or more. Validity's annual State of CRM Data Health research consistently finds that more than nine in ten businesses report data quality problems that affect revenue outcomes. Poor data does not just look untidy; it produces inaccurate forecasts and erodes the team's confidence in the system.

Pipeline accuracy

Whether the pipeline stages match the way deals actually move in the business, whether exit criteria are defined and consistently applied, and whether average deal age per stage is plausible or reflects deals being moved forward on optimism rather than evidence. This section compares the pipeline structure against the real sales process and identifies where stage drift is occurring.

Usage and adoption

Who logs in, how often, and what they actually record. A CRM that the team does not use consistently produces data that cannot be trusted regardless of how well it is configured. This section looks at login frequency, activity logging rates across calls, emails and meetings, and the points in the sales process where the team stops entering data.

Integration health

Which integrations are connected, which have broken without anyone noticing, and which data flows are missing. Email integration is the most common problem: if the CRM is not syncing email activity, the contact timeline is incomplete and reps lose context when it matters most.

Configuration and reporting

Whether the fields, pipeline views and reports in the CRM answer the questions leadership actually asks. A system can be correctly configured and still fail to answer the forecast question because the dashboard was built for a different process. This section checks whether the CRM as it currently stands matches what the business needs from it.

How the engagement works.

The audit runs over two to three weeks and involves no changes to the live system. It is read-only throughout.

The first step is access and intake. Read access to the CRM and a thirty-minute call with whoever manages the sales process: the founder, a sales manager, or a revenue operations person. The call covers the questions the team needs the CRM to answer, the problems that have already been noticed, and any recent changes to the sales process that the CRM configuration may not have kept up with.

The second step is analysis. The data is exported and reviewed against each of the five areas. If something needs clarifying mid-review, a short follow-up call resolves it without waiting for the report.

The third step is the report. A written document covering the findings in each area with a clear priority rating for every issue: fix immediately (affecting revenue or forecast accuracy), fix soon (affecting reporting quality), or review over time (lower impact). The document ends with a summary of the most likely root causes and a recommended order of work.

A one-hour call follows to walk through the findings, answer questions and agree next steps. Most teams decide their priorities within a week of that call.

Outcomes and what to expect next.

After the audit, three things become clear: what is actually in the CRM, why it is not working, and what to fix first.

Most fixes fall into one of three categories. Process changes the team can make themselves, usually around how activity is logged or how pipeline stages are used. Configuration changes that need admin access, such as updating field requirements, adjusting stage definitions, or cleaning up duplicate records at scale. Adoption issues that call for a short training session or a change to how the team is managed.

A small number of audits conclude that the platform is genuinely the wrong fit for the business. That conclusion comes with specific reasons: the business has outgrown what the current CRM can do, the integrations it needs do not exist, or the reporting requirements have changed significantly since the CRM was chosen. Where that is the finding, the next step is a proper selection process. The CRM implementation consultant page sets out what that looks like end to end.

Where the audit reveals an adoption problem rather than a configuration one, the fix is usually a combination of process clarity and a focused enablement session. If adoption has failed across the whole team over an extended period, the CRM adoption page covers the more structured approach for getting a team to use a system consistently after a poor start.

A clean, trusted CRM also matters at a funding or acquisition point. Investors and acquirers look at pipeline data as a proxy for business health. A pipeline where deals have not been updated in months, contacts have missing fields and the stage distribution makes no commercial sense signals something different from one where the data is trusted, the forecast is defensible and the records are current. An audit that leads to a clean-up changes that signal in a meaningful way.

Common questions.

What does a CRM audit cover?

A CRM audit covers five areas: data quality (duplicates, missing fields, stale records), pipeline accuracy (stage definitions, deal age, exit criteria), team usage (login rates, activity logging), integration health (which connections are live and which have broken), and configuration (whether the fields, stages and reports match the actual sales process).

How long does a CRM audit take?

Most CRM audits run two to three weeks from initial access to the written report. The first week covers data export and an intake call. The second is analysis. The report and walk-through call happen in week three. For a straightforward setup, sometimes faster; for a CRM with a long history and multiple integrations, occasionally a week longer.

Do I need a new CRM after an audit?

Rarely. In most cases the problem is process, configuration or adoption rather than the platform itself. A CRM audit will tell you clearly which it is. If the platform genuinely is the wrong fit, the report explains why against specific requirements. That conclusion is less common than most people expect before they go through the process.

What is the difference between a CRM audit and a CRM health check?

They are the same thing. Health check is the more informal term; audit implies a more structured output. Both involve reviewing the same areas: data quality, pipeline accuracy, usage, integrations and configuration. The output from this engagement is a written report with a prioritised list of fixes, not a verbal summary.

What happens after the audit report is delivered?

A one-hour call to walk through the findings, answer questions and agree next steps. After that, you decide which fixes to tackle first. Some teams action the process and adoption changes themselves. Configuration work needs someone with admin access. Where the fixes are substantial, Lauren can take on the remediation as a follow-on engagement.

Which CRM platforms do you audit?

HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive, Zoho CRM and most mainstream platforms. Read access is all that is needed for the audit; no changes are made to the live system during the review. If you are on a less common platform, ask on the discovery call. The methodology applies to any CRM that can export its data.

How much does a CRM audit cost?

Priced per engagement, based on the complexity of the setup, the number of integrations and the size of the data set. A short discovery call is the starting point: it covers what the CRM looks like and what you need the audit to answer, at which point a scope and fee are agreed.
