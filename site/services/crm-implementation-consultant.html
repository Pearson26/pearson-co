CRM implementation & selection

A CRM implementation consultant who manages every step from selection to go-live.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.16 June 20268 min read

Hiring a CRM implementation consultant means one person owns the full project: system selection, configuration, data migration, workflow build, training and go-live. Lauren Pearson works as a CRM implementation consultant for B2B founder-led teams that need hands-on delivery, not an account team.

When you hire a CRM implementation company, you hire an account manager first. That person takes your brief, translates it into a project plan, and passes it to the person who does the actual work, who you may never meet directly. When something changes mid-project (and something always changes), the information travels back through the account manager before it reaches the person who can act on it.

When you hire a CRM implementation consultant directly, you work with the person doing the work. They attend the first call, understand the business before any tool is configured, and are the same person still in the room when the go-live question comes up. No translation layer. No account manager. Just one person who knows the project from day one and is directly accountable for the result.

That matters most for founder-led teams with a specific sales process, where the CRM needs to fit the way the team actually works rather than a vendor's default workflow. It also matters when the scope might shift mid-project: a direct working relationship can adapt quickly, without a formal change-request process.

Who hires a CRM implementation consultant.

The businesses that benefit most from a consultant rather than a company tend to share a few characteristics.

The team is small enough that one person can understand the whole business in a short time, but large enough that the CRM needs to hold a real, structured sales process. Usually between five and fifty people. The sales motion is specific enough that a default CRM pipeline does not fit out of the box: a professional services firm with a long consultative sell, a SaaS business running a product-led and sales-led motion at the same time, a hospitality tech company opening a new market and needing the CRM ready before the sales team arrives.

The founder or revenue leader also wants to work directly with the person doing the work. Not because they distrust implementation companies in general, but because they know that the most important decisions on a CRM project are the ones that come up mid-project, not the ones agreed in the first meeting. When those decisions need to be made quickly, a direct line matters.

What a CRM implementation consultant does.

Six things, end to end.

  • Process and workflow mapping. Before the first field is configured, the sales process is documented: stages, exit criteria, handover points, reporting requirements. This is the foundation the CRM is built on. Configuring without it is what produces systems that do not match how anyone actually sells.
  • Platform selection (where the choice is not yet made). Selecting the right CRM against the process map rather than the feature list. For most founder-led B2B businesses, the realistic shortlist is HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive or Zoho, each with real trade-offs depending on team size, sales complexity and integration requirements.
  • Configuration and build. Pipeline stages, deal fields, contact properties, email integration, automations and permission levels, built to the agreed workflow. Not the vendor's defaults, not a template that looks close enough.
  • Data migration. Moving existing data (spreadsheets, an old CRM, or a combination) into the new system with the structure agreed in the process mapping phase. The Validity State of CRM Data Health report (2023) found that data quality problems are the most common cause of CRM adoption failures. Starting with trusted data is starting well.
  • Training. A session built around your team's actual workflow and the actions they will take every day, not a product tour of features they will never use. Followed by a written SOP for the process so the standard is documented for new starters rather than passed on informally.
  • Go-live and bedding-in. On hand for the first weeks after go-live so small adjustments are made fast. The first month is when usage habits form and when the small gaps between what was planned and what actually happens become visible. Being there to close them quickly makes a real difference to whether the system sticks.

Consultant vs implementation company: what actually changes.

The difference is not just cost, though a direct consultant rate is usually lower than an agency project fee of equivalent scope, because there is no account management overhead built in.

The difference is continuity and directness. An agency project passes through at least two people: the account manager and the person doing the configuration. If the scope changes, it goes back through that chain. In a direct engagement, a scope adjustment takes a conversation.

An agency implementation is often handed over once live. The person who did the configuration moves to their next project. A direct consultant relationship continues in whatever form is useful: a monthly check-in, a periodic configuration review when the process evolves, or a return engagement when the team grows and the system needs to scale with it.

Neither model is always better. For very large or very complex implementations with dozens of system integrations, regulated data requirements and hundreds of users, an agency with a dedicated team is often the right answer. For a founder-led business with a specific process and a hands-on founder, a consultant is usually more effective and costs less. The question is which situation you are in.

Outcomes and what a good implementation delivers.

Industry surveys consistently find that more than half of CRM projects fail to meet their original objectives. The causes are well documented: the team does not use the system, the pipeline data cannot be trusted, or the process was never mapped before the tool was configured. All three are preventable. None of them require a large budget to fix. They require a clear process first.

A good CRM implementation is not one that goes live on budget. It is one where, three months after go-live, the team logs their activity without being prompted, the pipeline report reflects what is actually in the business, and leadership trusts the data enough to make decisions from it. Those three things are the actual measure.

Common questions.

What is the difference between hiring a CRM consultant and a CRM implementation company?

With an implementation company, you have an account manager who translates between you and the person doing the actual configuration. With a consultant, you work directly with the person doing the work. That directness matters when the scope shifts mid-project, and it usually means a lower total cost because there is no account management overhead built into the fee.

Can one person manage a full CRM implementation?

Yes, for the size of business this model suits: typically 5 to 50 people with a single-team sales process. The scope that needs a dedicated team is a large enterprise with dozens of integrations, complex compliance requirements or hundreds of users across multiple markets. For most founder-led B2B businesses, one experienced consultant who knows the project from day one is more effective, not less.

Which CRM platforms do you work with?

The most common are HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive and Zoho CRM. Platform selection is scoped against the process map and reporting needs, not against the feature list, and I will tell you honestly if a platform is not the right fit. The selection decision is part of the engagement, not an assumed starting point.

How long does a CRM implementation with a consultant take?

For a B2B team of 5 to 25 people with a straightforward sales process, typically 6 to 10 weeks from the first call to go-live. The main variable is data migration: a clean spreadsheet takes a week; a large or inconsistent dataset from an old CRM takes two or three. Timeline is agreed once the data situation is understood.

What happens if our process changes after go-live?

That is one of the reasons the direct consultant model works well for founder-led businesses. When a process changes, a configuration update takes a call rather than a formal change request and a quoted addition. The working relationship continues in whatever form is useful: a quarterly review, a return engagement when the team grows, or a specific project when a new tool needs integrating.

Do you work with businesses outside the UK?

Yes. The businesses I work with are based in the UK, the UAE, across Europe and in the US. CRM implementation does not require physical presence: process mapping, configuration, training and go-live support all work remotely. Where a team prefers an in-person training session, that can be arranged.

Ready to implement a CRM your team will actually use?

Bring your current sales process and we will map the workflow on the call, agree the platform if it is not yet chosen, and confirm what the project looks like before any work begins.
