CRM implementation & selection

CRM implementation services built around how your team works.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.16 June 20268 min read

The Pearson Co.'s CRM implementation services cover system selection, configuration, data migration, workflow build and team training for B2B businesses. The focus is on workflow and reporting logic before any tool setup, so the CRM your team receives is one they will actually use.

The most common reason a CRM implementation fails is not a bad choice of software. It is a project managed as a technical deployment rather than a people-and-process one: the system is configured to a default template, fields are added for the sake of having them, and training covers how to click buttons rather than how the workflow connects to the team's day. The CRM goes live, usage drops off, and six months later there are two systems running in parallel: the CRM and a spreadsheet.

CRM implementation services from The Pearson Co. start with the workflow, not the tool. Before any platform is configured, the sales and account management process is mapped, the reporting requirements are agreed, and the data that actually matters is identified. The tool is then built to match that, not the other way round.

If you want to understand what to look for when choosing a platform, the CRM implementation and selection page covers the full picture of what makes an implementation succeed or fail. This page is for businesses that have chosen their CRM, or are close to choosing it, and want to know what a properly run implementation project actually involves.

Who CRM implementation services are built for.

This service suits B2B and SaaS founder-led businesses in one of four situations.

  • First-time CRM implementation. You have been running on spreadsheets or a mix of tools and you need a single system the team will actually log in to. You want it set up to fit your sales process, not reconfigured after the fact.
  • Replacing a CRM that never worked. A previous implementation went live but the team stopped using it, or the data cannot be trusted, or the reporting tells leadership nothing useful. Starting again with the process mapped first.
  • Preparing for team growth. You have just hired a sales team, a revenue operations lead or a VP of Sales, and you need the system ready before they start. A CRM that is not set up before the team arrives means the first few months are spent building backwards rather than selling.
  • Post-acquisition or merger tidy-up. Two businesses are running two different CRMs and the data needs to be consolidated into one system with a consistent pipeline and reporting structure.

This is not the right fit for very large enterprises with complex multi-system integration requirements or regulated data environments that need specialist compliance work alongside the implementation. That is a different scope at a different cost.

What is included in the service.

Six components, end to end.

  • Process mapping before configuration. Before touching the CRM, the sales process, account management workflow and handover points between teams are documented. This is what gets configured into the system, not the vendor's default pipeline. Getting this right is the whole job. The configuration is straightforward once the process is clear.
  • CRM selection (where the platform is not yet chosen). If the choice is still open, selection is scoped against the process map and the reporting needs, not against a feature checklist. The right CRM for a 10-person professional services firm is not the same one as for a 50-person SaaS team, and the vendor comparison guides do not tell you the difference clearly.
  • Platform configuration. Build-out of the agreed workflow: pipelines, stages, fields, automations, permission levels, email integration and reporting. Done to what the team actually needs, not to what the onboarding wizard suggests.
  • Data migration. Moving contact records, deal history and any other relevant data from spreadsheets, an old CRM, or a mix of both, into the new platform with the data model agreed in the process mapping phase. The Validity State of CRM Data Health report (2023) found that poor data quality is the single most cited barrier to CRM adoption. Starting with trusted data is starting well.
  • Team training. A session built around your actual workflow, your actual pipeline stages and the actions the team will take every day. Followed by a written SOP for the process, so the standard is documented for new starters from day one, not passed on by word of mouth.
  • Go-live support. On hand for the first two to three weeks after go-live so questions get answered quickly and small configuration adjustments do not wait for a formal change request. The first month is when usage habits form.

Outcomes and what a good implementation looks like.

The measure of a successful implementation is not "we went live on time". It is three things: the team logs their work in the CRM without being asked, leadership can pull a pipeline report they trust, and the data in the system matches what the sales team says out loud. Those three things are what a good implementation delivers. They are also what most implementations fail to deliver.

Industry surveys consistently find that more than half of CRM projects fail to meet their original objectives. The consistent causes are the same across most of them: the team does not use the system, the data cannot be trusted, or the process was never mapped before the tool was configured. Addressing all three from the start is what changes the outcome.

Common questions.

Which CRMs do you implement?

The most common platforms in the businesses I work with are HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive and Zoho CRM. The right choice depends on team size, sales process complexity and reporting needs. Selection is scoped against your process map before any platform is chosen, and I will tell you honestly if a platform is not the right fit rather than defaulting to the one I know best.

How long does a CRM implementation project take?

Typically 6 to 12 weeks for a straightforward implementation with a team of 5 to 20 people. The main variables are data migration complexity, the number of systems being integrated and the number of people in the training sessions. Larger teams or more complex configurations take longer and are scoped at the start of the project.

How much do CRM implementation services cost?

Cost depends on scope: the size of the team, the complexity of the process and whether the data migration is simple or involves cleaning a large existing dataset. Scope and price are agreed at the discovery call once the process map is understood. There is no standard rate card because no two implementations are the same.

What if the team does not adopt the CRM after go-live?

Adoption is built into the implementation, not added at the end. The process starts with the workflow the team actually uses, configures the CRM to that, and trains people on their own process rather than the software in the abstract. That said, if team incentives do not align with CRM use, no implementation solves that on its own, and it is worth naming the issue early rather than discovering it after go-live.

Can you migrate our data from a spreadsheet or an old CRM?

Yes. Migration is included in the implementation scope. The first step is understanding what data you have, what condition it is in and what structure it needs to be in for the new system. Starting with clean, trusted data on day one is worth the time it takes, because bad data means the pipeline reporting cannot be relied on from week one.

Do you work with small teams or only larger businesses?

Most of the businesses I work with are between 5 and 50 people: small enough that one person can understand the whole business quickly, large enough that the CRM needs to hold a real, structured sales process. Very small teams with simple processes often do not need a full implementation, and I will say so rather than selling a project that is bigger than the need.
