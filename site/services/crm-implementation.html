CRM implementation & selection

CRM implementation that your team will actually use.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.16 June 202610 min read

CRM implementation is choosing, configuring and rolling out a CRM so a team uses it daily and leadership trusts the numbers it produces. It is more change project than software project. Most fail on adoption, not features, so the work that matters is process design, clean data and a planned rollout.

Most founders I speak to have already bought a CRM. Often two. The licence renews every year, a handful of deals sit in it, and the real pipeline still lives in a spreadsheet on someone's laptop. The system is not the problem. The way it was implemented is.

Good CRM implementation is the work of choosing the right system, setting it up around how your team actually sells, and rolling it out so people use it without being nagged. Get that right and you have one reliable view of the pipeline, forecasts you can act on, and a sales team that spends less time on admin. Get it wrong and you have an expensive contact list nobody opens.

This page sets out how I approach CRM selection and implementation for founder-led and scaling teams in the UK, what an engagement involves, and how to avoid the failure that quietly sinks most projects.

What CRM implementation actually means.

A CRM, or customer relationship management system, is the single place your business records every prospect, customer and deal, and the activity around them. CRM implementation is everything that turns an empty piece of software into a system your team relies on. It breaks into five parts.

  • Selection. Defining what you need and choosing the system that fits, rather than the one with the best demo.
  • Configuration. Building the pipeline stages, fields, automations and permissions around your real sales process.
  • Data migration. Moving contacts and deals across cleanly, so you start with data people can trust rather than a copy of the old mess.
  • Integration. Connecting the CRM to your email, calendar, marketing tools, finance system and website so it stops being a silo.
  • Adoption. Training, rollout and the first weeks of live use, where habits are either formed or lost.

The first four are technical and finite. The fifth decides whether any of it was worth doing. That is why I treat implementation as a change project that happens to involve software, not the other way round.

Why most CRM projects fail on adoption.

The pattern is consistent. A team picks a CRM in a hurry, a reseller configures it to look impressive, data gets dumped in, and everyone is told to start using it from Monday. Within a month the sales reps have drifted back to spreadsheets, the data is half-complete, and the founder cannot get a straight answer on what is going to close this quarter.

None of that is a software fault. It is a design and rollout fault. A few causes come up again and again.

  • The system mirrors the org chart, not the sales process. If logging a deal takes fourteen fields and three clicks that do not match how the rep actually works, they will not do it.
  • Nobody owns the rollout. Implementation is treated as an IT task that ends at go live, when the real work starts the day people log in.
  • Dirty data on day one. If the first report a rep sees is wrong, they stop trusting the system, and trust is very hard to win back.
  • No reason for the rep to bother. If the CRM only serves management reporting and gives the rep nothing in return, it feels like surveillance, not a tool.

Fixing adoption is not a training problem you solve with a one-hour session. It runs through every earlier decision: how you scope the system, how you design the process, and how you stage the rollout. This is closely tied to CRM adoption as an ongoing discipline, and it is the reason I spend as much time on people as on the platform.

How I lead CRM selection for founder-led teams.

Selection comes before any software. I am vendor-neutral, which means I have no reseller margin riding on which system you pick. The job is to find the one that fits your sales motion, team and budget, then move on quickly so the energy goes into the build.

The work runs in a clear order.

  1. Map the sales process first. Before comparing tools, we get clear on how a deal actually moves from first contact to closed. This is where process mapping earns its place, because you cannot configure a CRM around a process nobody has written down.
  2. Write the requirements. A short, ranked list of what the CRM must do, should do and need not do. Reporting needs, integrations, the number of users, and any UK-specific concerns such as GDPR and where data is held.
  3. Shortlist, not a beauty parade. For most founder teams the realistic shortlist is two or three systems. HubSpot, Pipedrive and Salesforce cover the majority of cases, with the right answer depending on whether you lead with marketing, with outbound sales, or with a more involved enterprise motion.
  4. Score against requirements, then decide. We test the shortlist against the ranked list and the total cost over three years, including the seats you will add as you grow, not just today's price. Then we choose and stop looking.

There is no single best CRM, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling one. The right system is the one your team will use, that produces numbers your leadership trusts, and that you will not outgrow in eighteen months.

What a CRM implementation engagement includes.

Once the system is chosen, implementation follows a defined sequence. I work hands-on through it with your team rather than handing over a document and disappearing.

Process and configuration

We turn the mapped sales process into pipeline stages with clear entry and exit criteria, a tight set of required fields, and sensible defaults. Less is more here. A rep should be able to log a deal in seconds, and every field should either help the rep or feed a report someone genuinely uses. Where repetitive admin can be removed, we set up CRM automation so the system does the chasing, the reminders and the handoffs.

Data migration and clean-up

We move your contacts and deals across, de-duplicate, and agree what counts as a complete record. Starting clean matters more than starting complete. It is better to migrate a smaller set of trustworthy data than to import years of half-finished spreadsheets and inherit the mess.

Integration with your stack

The CRM connects to email and calendar, to your marketing and website forms, and where it makes sense to finance and support tools, so a customer's full history sits in one place. This is the difference between a CRM that reflects reality and one that always seems a step behind it.

Reporting and dashboards

We build the views leadership needs from the start: pipeline by stage, forecast, win rates and activity. Getting reporting right early is what lets you run the business from the CRM rather than from a spreadsheet someone updates on a Friday. If you want to go deeper here, this connects directly to sales dashboards.

Rollout, training and adoption

Training is role-specific and short, then I am there through the first weeks of live use, when the questions and the quiet workarounds appear. We name an internal owner, set a small number of habits that matter, and check the data weekly until using the CRM is simply how the team works. This is the stage most projects skip and most projects regret skipping.

What good CRM implementation is worth commercially.

This is not a tidy-up exercise. A CRM that gets used changes how the business runs.

  • A pipeline number you can trust. Forecasting stops being a guess. You can see what is real, what is stalled, and where to put effort this month.
  • Less time on admin. When the system removes manual logging and chasing, reps spend more of the week actually selling.
  • Nothing falls through the cracks. Follow-ups happen, handoffs are clean, and warm leads stop going cold because someone forgot.
  • Decisions based on data, not anecdote. Leadership runs on the same numbers, and those numbers hold up in a board meeting.

A CRM is also the foundation for the wider operating system of a growing business. Once the data is reliable, it feeds everything from forecasting to compensation. If you are thinking beyond the tool itself, CRM implementation sits inside a broader revenue operations approach that aligns sales, marketing and customer success around one source of truth.

The mistakes I see most often.

Before you start, it helps to know the traps. These are the ones that turn up most often when I am asked to rescue an implementation that has stalled.

  • Buying before scoping. A system gets bought on the back of a demo, then the team tries to bend their process to fit it. Requirements come first, software second.
  • Over-building at launch. Twenty custom fields and a dozen automations on day one. Start lean, prove the habit, then add complexity once people are actually in the system.
  • Treating go live as the finish line. Go live is the start of adoption, not the end of the project. The first month decides whether it sticks.
  • No single owner. When the CRM belongs to everyone it belongs to no one. One named internal owner keeps the data clean and the standards held.

None of these are about the software being wrong. They are about sequence and ownership, which is exactly where independent help changes the outcome.

Common questions.

What does CRM implementation involve?

CRM implementation involves choosing the right system, configuring it around how your team actually sells, migrating clean data, integrating the tools you already use, training people and supporting them through the first weeks of live use. It is part software project and part change project, and the change part is where most of the value sits.

How long does a CRM implementation take?

A focused implementation for a founder-led team usually runs six to twelve weeks from selection to go live, depending on how many integrations and how much data are involved. Adoption support then continues for a further four to eight weeks so the habit sticks rather than fading after launch.

Why do most CRM projects fail?

Most CRM projects fail on adoption, not software. The system gets configured to mirror an org chart rather than the sales process, the data is messy, and nobody owns the rollout. Sales reps quietly keep working in spreadsheets, and the reporting nobody trusts gets ignored.

Which CRM should a UK founder choose?

There is no single best CRM. The right choice depends on your sales motion, team size, budget and the tools you already run. For most UK founder-led teams the shortlist sits between HubSpot, Pipedrive and Salesforce. Selection should follow your written requirements, not a vendor demo.

Do I need a CRM implementation consultant?

If your team has tried a CRM before and abandoned it, or you cannot get a reliable pipeline number from your current setup, an independent CRM implementation consultant pays for itself. The value is in vendor-neutral selection, process design and the adoption work that resellers rarely do well.

How much does CRM implementation cost?

Consulting fees for a founder-team implementation typically range from a few thousand to low tens of thousands of pounds depending on scope, separate from the CRM licence itself. The larger cost of getting it wrong is months of lost reporting and a team that has stopped trusting the system.
