Fractional sales leadership

A fractional CRO who aligns the whole revenue motion, not just the sales team.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.19 June 20267 min read

A fractional chief revenue officer (fractional CRO) leads a company's full revenue function part-time: sales, marketing and customer success, aligned around a single process and set of metrics. Lauren Pearson works as a fractional CRO for B2B businesses that need unified commercial leadership without the cost of a full-time executive hire.

The gap that makes a fractional CRO necessary is usually visible before anyone names it. Sales blames marketing for poor lead quality. Marketing blames sales for not following up on the leads it generates. Customer success is tracking its own metrics, disconnected from both. Revenue is growing but it feels harder than it should, and there is no single person who owns the full number.

A fractional CRO is the person who owns the full number. Not just the sales pipeline, but the whole commercial motion: how leads are generated and qualified, how they are converted, how customers are retained and expanded, and how all of that is reported to the board. Two to three days a week, with the depth and accountability of an executive hire, without the full-time cost.

The fractional sales leadership page covers the wider model. This page is specific to the fractional CRO engagement and what makes it different from a fractional sales director or VP Sales.

Who a fractional CRO is for.

The fractional CRO role suits businesses where the commercial challenge spans multiple functions, not just the sales team. If the problem is specifically the sales pipeline or team management, a fractional sales director is the narrower, more appropriate engagement. The CRO scope is right when the issue is structural: functions operating independently of each other, no single owner of the revenue motion, and reporting that shows activity but not direction.

Specific situations where this engagement fits:

  • Your business has separate heads of sales, marketing and customer success but no one who owns the full revenue motion and is accountable for how they connect.
  • You have raised a funding round on founder-driven revenue and now need to build the commercial infrastructure to hit the growth targets without the founder at the centre of every deal and every decision.
  • You are expanding into a new market, particularly the US or the Middle East, and need strategic commercial leadership for the entry without a permanent hire in a geography that may need reshaping once the market is understood.
  • You are preparing for a Series A or B and investors want to see a defined revenue strategy, unified pipeline reporting and a credible commercial leader before they commit.

The fractional CRO is not the right model if you need someone primarily to generate and close business. That is a sales hire. The CRO role is about architecture: designing the system, aligning the teams, setting the metrics, and building the process that lets the business grow without constant founder intervention.

What a fractional CRO includes.

A fractional CRO engagement covers the full revenue function, not just one part of it.

Revenue architecture. Defining how your revenue model works: which channels, which customer segments, what the pipeline looks like from first touch to retention, and where the bottlenecks are. For most businesses, this starts with mapping what currently happens versus what should happen, and the gap between the two is usually larger than expected. Forrester's B2B Revenue Leadership research found that companies with a unified revenue architecture grow 2.4 times faster than those with siloed commercial functions.

Sales and marketing alignment. Setting shared definitions (what is a qualified lead, what is a working opportunity, when does marketing hand off to sales), shared metrics and a shared reporting cadence. This is often the single change that makes the biggest immediate difference. Without a shared definition of a qualified lead, sales and marketing are measuring different things and attributing different causes to the same result.

CRM and RevOps infrastructure. Making sure the CRM, marketing automation and reporting tools support the revenue architecture rather than just recording activity. If the CRM needs a diagnostic before strategy work makes sense, a CRM audit is the right starting point. For the full picture of how revenue systems connect, the revenue operations page describes the infrastructure work in detail.

Customer success integration. Net revenue retention is the clearest measure of a healthy revenue motion. A fractional CRO who works across the full function can identify where customers are churning or not expanding, and whether the root cause sits in onboarding, product fit, support quality or expectations set during the sales process. Fixing a retention problem by going back into the sales motion is one of the most direct levers a CRO has.

Board reporting and investor communication. Building the revenue dashboard that lets the board track progress, and presenting it with the context to interpret it. This is where many growth-stage businesses have the clearest gap: the data exists but no one has built the view of the business that leadership can act on. A fractional CRO who also presents to the board gives investors a credible commercial voice alongside the founder.

How the engagement works.

A fractional CRO engagement runs on two to three days a week, with a six-month minimum commitment to allow for strategy, implementation and measurable results. The structure is consistent:

Months one and two: diagnostic and architecture. Understanding the current state of the revenue function across all three commercial teams, identifying the biggest gaps, and agreeing the priorities with the founder or board. This is not a reporting phase: it includes direct work with the marketing, sales and customer success teams to understand what is working and what is not.

Months three and four: implementation. Building the processes, metrics and reporting the business needs. This is usually when the CRM and RevOps infrastructure changes happen, and when the team cadences get established: pipeline reviews, marketing-to-sales handoffs, customer success check-ins and board reporting cycles.

Months five and six: stabilisation and handoff planning. The revenue function should be operating more independently by this point. The question shifts to what ongoing leadership looks like: a lighter fractional commitment, a full-time CRO hire, or an internal promotion from within the team.

Outcomes and what a good engagement delivers.

The clearest measure of a fractional CRO engagement is whether the business has a revenue function that operates without constant founder involvement, reported through a set of metrics the board trusts.

Specific outcomes clients have asked for and received: a unified pipeline from first touch to close to retention; a CRM and reporting setup that gives leadership a real-time view of the commercial motion; a marketing-to-sales handoff both teams follow consistently; and a commercial strategy for a new market entry that did not require the founder to personally own every early deal.

The engagements that produce the most durable results are those where the founder commits to the process change alongside the hiring decision. A fractional CRO who designs a new pipeline review process is only effective if the sales team is asked to follow it. A new shared definition of a qualified lead only matters if marketing changes how it measures campaign success. The fractional CRO designs and drives the change, but the founder has to back it.

If you are still deciding between a fractional CRO and a fractional sales director, the fractional sales leadership page sets out the full comparison. The short version: if the problem is in sales alone, the sales director engagement is more focused and typically faster to show results. If the problem spans the commercial function, the CRO scope is the right answer.

Common questions.

What is a fractional CRO?

A fractional chief revenue officer (CRO) is a senior revenue leader who works with a business part-time, typically two to three days a week. They own the full revenue motion: sales, marketing and customer success, aligned around shared metrics and a single process. It is a strategic and architectural role, not an individual sales contributor role.

What is the difference between a fractional CRO and a fractional sales director?

A fractional sales director focuses on the sales team and the pipeline. A fractional CRO spans the full revenue function: sales, marketing and customer success together. The CRO role is right when the commercial challenge sits across multiple teams. If the problem is specifically the sales process or team management, a fractional sales director is often the more appropriate engagement.

How much does a fractional CRO cost?

A fractional CRO engagement costs a fraction of a full-time CRO salary, scaled to the number of days per week. Full-time CRO salaries at B2B scale-ups range from £150,000 to £250,000 in the UK and $200,000 to $350,000 in the US. A two-day-a-week fractional engagement is substantially less, with no employer NI, no recruitment fee and no notice period. Contact The Pearson Co. for a scope-specific quote.

How long does a fractional CRO engagement last?

The minimum useful commitment is six months, to allow time for diagnosis, implementation and measurable results. Many engagements run for 12 months or longer, particularly during a growth phase or market expansion. The engagement typically ends when the business is ready to hire a full-time CRO or has an internal leader ready to take on the role permanently.

Does a fractional CRO work with the board?

Yes. Board reporting on revenue performance is part of the role. A fractional CRO builds the revenue dashboard, presents the pipeline and commercial strategy, and takes accountability for the commercial targets alongside the founder. This is often one of the most valued parts of the engagement: having a credible, experienced revenue leader who can answer investor questions directly.

Is a fractional CRO right for a Series A business?

It can be, particularly for a business that has raised its A round on founder-driven revenue and needs to build the commercial infrastructure before the B round. The classic mistake at Series A is hiring a full-time CRO before the revenue process is documented and the team is in place. A fractional CRO who builds the foundation first makes the eventual full-time hire significantly more likely to succeed.
