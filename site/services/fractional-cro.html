Fractional sales leadership

A fractional CRO who aligns sales, marketing and customer success around one revenue number.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.18 June 20267 min read

A fractional chief revenue officer takes ownership of the entire revenue function: sales, marketing and customer success. They work part-time on a monthly retainer, setting the revenue strategy, aligning the teams and building the commercial infrastructure the business needs to grow predictably. Lauren Pearson works in this role for scaling B2B businesses.

A VP of Sales fixes the sales team. A fractional CRO fixes the whole revenue engine.

That distinction matters. When the sales team is working but growth has still stalled, the issue is rarely inside the sales function itself. It is in the handover from marketing: leads that do not convert because they were never the right fit. Or in customer success: revenue that comes in and then churns before it compounds. Or in the disconnect between what marketing says and what sales is actually selling. A Chief Revenue Officer takes responsibility for all three functions and the alignment between them, not just one part of the picture. The broader context for fractional senior leadership is on the fractional sales leadership page, including when a fractional sales director is the better fit for a business focused on the sales function specifically.

Who a fractional CRO is for.

The fractional CRO model suits a specific stage of business. The most common situation is a SaaS or B2B services company with between $1 million and $10 million in annual recurring or project revenue. The business has grown past the point where the founder can personally oversee all three revenue functions, but has not reached the scale where a full-time CRO with a US base salary above $250,000 (Radford, 2023) is financially justified.

Three patterns come up repeatedly in the businesses that benefit most.

The first is misalignment between marketing and sales. Marketing generates leads that sales considers low quality. Sales needs case studies, segment-specific content or a tighter definition of the ideal customer that marketing is not building. Both teams are working hard but not on the same problem. Revenue falls through the gap between them. Neither team can fix this from inside their own function; it needs someone who owns both.

The second is a retention problem running alongside a growth one. New revenue is coming in but churn is consuming it. Customer success does not have a clear renewal process, the data to identify at-risk accounts early, or the mandate to work closely enough with sales to catch problems before they become exits. The business is running hard to stand still. Fixing new revenue without fixing retention is building into a leaking bucket.

The third is a Series A or B fundraise. Investors want a credible revenue leader who can explain the model, defend the assumptions and give a clear account of what is driving growth and what is not. A fractional CRO can step into that conversation, and once the round closes, help hire the permanent executive into an organisation that already has revenue infrastructure in place. Gartner noted in 2022 that by 2025, 75% of the highest-growth companies globally would deploy the CRO function. For businesses that need the function but cannot yet justify the permanent hire, fractional is the practical alternative.

What is included.

A fractional CRO engagement covers five areas.

Revenue audit. The starting point is an honest view of the current revenue picture: where deals originate, what conversion rates look like at each stage of the funnel, what the retention rate is, and where the biggest gaps are between what is in the pipeline and what actually closes. Most businesses know their headline revenue figure but do not have a clear view of how each channel performs or where revenue is being lost before it reaches the close. The audit establishes that picture before any changes are made.

Go-to-market alignment. Marketing, sales and customer success need to work from the same model. That means a shared ideal customer profile, agreed handover criteria between marketing and sales, and a common definition of what a successful customer looks like at each stage of their relationship with the business. Without alignment, each team optimises for its own metrics rather than for shared revenue, and the results show up in missed targets and finger-pointing between functions.

Pipeline and forecasting. A reliable revenue forecast depends on clean data, clear conversion rates and honest deal qualification across the whole funnel, not just in the sales stages. The fractional CRO sets the forecasting discipline across the team and builds the reporting infrastructure that makes the number one the business can stand behind in a board meeting or an investor conversation.

Retention and expansion. Reducing churn and growing revenue from existing customers is usually the highest-return commercial activity available to a scaling business. The fractional CRO designs the retention and expansion process: the renewal cadence, the health score model, the playbook for turning a renewal conversation into an expansion conversation. These are the mechanisms that make revenue compound rather than reset every quarter.

Revenue infrastructure. CRM configuration, reporting dashboards, team structure and handover processes between functions. The infrastructure is what makes strategy repeatable. Without it, the approach depends on the specific individuals in the roles rather than on a process that survives personnel changes. How the engagement works.

A fractional CRO engagement runs in three phases.

The first 30 days are a revenue audit. Lauren maps the current funnel from top to bottom: lead sources, conversion rates at each stage, average deal size, close rates, churn rate and expansion metrics. This includes conversations with the marketing, sales and customer success teams to understand the practical reality alongside the data. The purpose is to agree on the actual state of the revenue function before deciding what to change.

Days 30 to 90 are the build. This is where the shared revenue model is designed, the CRM is configured to reflect the whole funnel, the forecasting cadence is established, and the handover processes between teams are agreed and written down. These 60 days are usually the most intensive part of the engagement. The decisions made here set the standard the team operates to going forward.

From month four onwards, the engagement runs on a monthly retainer: one to two days per week, focused on the pipeline review, team coaching, reporting and any new commercial initiative the business is working on. The retainer can be scaled for periods when more capacity is needed (a product launch, a new market entry, a fundraise process) and scaled back as the organisation builds its own capability.

Most engagements run six to eighteen months. Some businesses use the arrangement to build the revenue infrastructure and then operate independently. Others continue with a retained fractional CRO alongside a growing in-house team, using the engagement as a senior strategic resource rather than an operational one. Either is a valid outcome; the right one depends on what the business needs at that point.

Outcomes and what the business gets.

The practical outputs are an aligned revenue team, a reliable forecast and a documented revenue model the business can execute without the fractional leader present in every meeting.

The specific things built during the engagement include: a documented go-to-market model, a shared ideal customer profile, a CRM configured to reflect the full revenue funnel, a pipeline and renewal dashboard that all three teams work from, and a retention process that identifies at-risk accounts before they exit. These are deliverables, not recommendations. The work produces things that exist in the business after the engagement ends.

The fractional model is also honest about its end state. Part of the job is building a team and a set of processes that can run without the fractional leader. When that happens, the engagement has succeeded. The business either continues independently or hires a permanent CRO into a role that already has working infrastructure, a functioning team and a track record. Recruiting a full-time CRO into that position is a different and significantly more straightforward exercise than recruiting one to fix something that is not working.

Common questions.

What is a fractional CRO?

A fractional CRO (Chief Revenue Officer) is a senior revenue leader who works with your business part-time on a monthly retainer. They take responsibility for the entire revenue function: sales, marketing and customer success. Unlike a fractional sales director who focuses on the sales team, the fractional CRO owns the full revenue picture and the alignment between all three functions.

What is the difference between a fractional CRO and a fractional sales director?

A fractional sales director focuses on the sales team: process, pipeline, coaching and forecasting. A fractional CRO takes a wider remit across sales, marketing and customer success, aligning them to a shared revenue model. If the problem is inside the sales function, a fractional sales director is the right fit. If the problem spans the whole revenue engine, the fractional CRO is the correct answer.

How much does a fractional CRO cost?

A fractional CRO is billed on a monthly retainer tied to the days per week agreed at the start. A full-time CRO in the US typically earns $250,000 to $350,000 in base salary before equity. A fractional engagement for one to two days per week costs significantly less, making senior revenue leadership accessible at a stage where a permanent hire is not yet justified.

At what revenue stage does a fractional CRO make sense?

The most common fit is a B2B SaaS or services business with $1 million to $10 million in annual recurring or project revenue. At that stage, the business has grown past what the founder can personally oversee across all revenue channels, but has not reached the scale to justify a full-time CRO hire at the salary that role commands.

How long does a fractional CRO engagement typically run?

Most engagements run six to eighteen months. Some businesses use the fractional arrangement to build the revenue infrastructure and then continue independently. Others retain a fractional CRO alongside a growing team as a senior strategic resource. Either is a valid outcome; the duration is driven by what is useful rather than a fixed term in the contract.

Can a fractional CRO also handle our CRM and reporting infrastructure?

Yes. CRM configuration and revenue reporting are a standard part of the engagement. A revenue strategy without the infrastructure to track it is a plan, not an operating reality. If a new CRM implementation is needed as part of the build, that can be scoped as part of the engagement or run alongside it.
