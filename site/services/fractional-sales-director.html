Fractional sales leadership

A fractional sales director who owns the pipeline, not just the process.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.20 June 20267 min read

A fractional sales director is a senior sales leader working part-time, typically one to two days a week, on a monthly retainer. They own the sales strategy, manage the team and drive the pipeline without the cost of a permanent hire. Lauren Pearson works as a fractional sales director for B2B founder-led and scaling businesses globally.

A fractional sales director gives a founder-led business a senior sales leader who owns the function, part-time. They run the strategy, manage the reps and drive the pipeline, without requiring the cost or commitment of a full-time hire. Most founder-led businesses that reach this point have the same underlying problem: it is not a lack of salespeople, it is a lack of the person who runs them.

The reps are in place. The CRM has some version of a pipeline. Deals are moving, but slowly, inconsistently and not reliably enough to forecast. What is missing is the senior layer: someone who sets the process, coaches the team, holds the number and makes sure the data can be trusted. A fractional sales director fills that gap at a cost and commitment level that matches where the business actually is.

Who this is for.

The founders who benefit most from a fractional sales director tend to share one trait: they are still the best salesperson on the team, or at least the one who closes the important deals, and they cannot work out how to change that without being in every meeting.

A fractional sales director is the right move when:

  • You have one to six salespeople but no one with formal ownership of the function above rep level.
  • You are still personally closing the majority of significant deals and the business depends on your calendar.
  • Reps are underperforming but you cannot diagnose why: whether it is the people, the process, the CRM discipline or some combination.
  • You are planning to add one or two more reps and want a proper process in place before they join.
  • Investors or a board have asked who owns the sales number, and the honest answer is still you.
  • You want a full-time VP of sales eventually but not yet, and you do not want to leave the function unled in the meantime.

It is not the right fit if you have no product-market fit yet: a fractional sales director cannot create demand that does not exist. It also does not replace a full-time leader if you have a large team that needs daily management. Between those two points, which is where most scaling B2B businesses sit, it is often the most effective way to add senior sales ownership.

What is included.

A fractional sales director does the work of the role, part-time. That is the distinction between this and a coach or an advisor: they do not review and recommend, they take ownership and manage.

In practice, the work covers five areas:

  • Sales strategy and ICP. Defining the ideal customer profile precisely, setting the sales motion (outbound, inbound, partner-led or a combination), agreeing the territory or segment focus, and setting targets the team can be held to.
  • Pipeline and process. Building the stage definitions, qualifying criteria and forecast discipline. This includes getting the CRM to reflect the actual sales process, so the pipeline report can be trusted and the forecast is based on evidence rather than optimism.
  • Coaching and performance management. Weekly one-to-ones with reps, deal reviews, call feedback and managing performance issues when they arise. The coaching is specific to your process and your customers, not a generic sales methodology applied from outside.
  • Hiring. Writing the job specification for the next rep, setting the interview process, assessing candidates and building an onboarding plan. Most founder-led businesses have not documented what good looks like for a rep in their specific context; building that is part of the engagement.
  • Reporting. Setting up the dashboards and the cadence of reviews so you and the team can see pipeline coverage, conversion rates, deal velocity and forecast without waiting for quarter end. Clean sales dashboards are how a part-time sales leader stays across the team without being in every deal.

How the engagement works.

Most engagements move through three phases. The balance of time in each shifts depending on what the business needs most at the start.

Phase one: diagnose (weeks one to three). The first three weeks are spent understanding the business before building anything. That means reviewing the pipeline data, listening to calls or sitting in on deal reviews, and talking to the existing team about where deals are won and lost. You get a clear, honest assessment of the sales function as it stands: what is working, what is not and what needs to change first.

Phase two: build (months two to four). The main delivery phase. The strategy is set, the process is built into the CRM, team coaching begins and any hiring work starts. The heaviest phase in terms of time, and the one where the most change happens. A typical cadence is two days a week, with a weekly pipeline review and monthly business review built in from the start.

Phase three: embed and hand over (month four onwards). The system runs. The focus shifts to coaching the team to run it without regular input, tightening the forecast, and either tapering to a lighter oversight retainer or briefing a full-time hire who takes over. The handover is a specific, planned event. If the plan is to bring in a full-time sales director, I can help write the job specification and sit on the final round of interviews.

Because much of a sales director's impact lands in the CRM and reporting layer, this work often connects naturally to broader revenue operations. A clean pipeline, a reliable forecast and clear handoffs between marketing and sales are what let a part-time leader manage a team effectively without being in every conversation.

Outcomes and proof.

The test of a good fractional sales director engagement is what the function looks like when it ends, or when it transitions to a full-time hire. By that point:

  • The sales process is documented and the CRM reflects it accurately.
  • Reps can close deals without the founder in the room.
  • The pipeline report can be trusted enough to inform a board discussion.
  • There is a hiring playbook for the next rep and a clear view of what good performance looks like in this specific business.
  • The forecast is based on data, not on who is having a good month.

The honest test is simple. If the founder stepped away from sales for thirty days, would the number hold? If the answer is still no at the end of the engagement, the engagement has not finished.

Common questions.

What is the difference between a fractional sales director and a fractional head of sales?

They describe the same role. Fractional head of sales, fractional VP of sales and fractional sales director all mean a senior sales leader working part-time, owning the sales function. The title matters less than the remit: are they responsible for strategy, process, team performance and the number, or are they advising on those things? The former is a fractional leader; the latter is a coach or advisor.

How many days a week does a fractional sales director work?

The most common arrangement is one to two days a week, depending on the size of the team and the phase of the engagement. During the initial diagnostic and main build phase, two days a week is typical. Once the process is running and the team is managing itself, one day a week is usually enough to maintain oversight and coaching.

How much does a fractional sales director cost?

A fractional sales director is billed on a monthly retainer tied to the number of days a week they work. That is significantly below the all-in cost of a full-time director once you add salary, bonus and on-costs, and the commitment is month-to-month or fixed-term rather than permanent employment. The exact figure depends on scope and days; book a call to discuss your situation.

Is a fractional sales director the same as a VP of sales?

A VP of sales is a full-time role with a management structure around it. A fractional sales director does the same strategic and management work but part-time, on a retainer, without permanent employment. The outcomes are similar in terms of what gets built; the difference is cost and commitment. For a business not yet ready for a full-time VP hire, fractional is often the more appropriate model.

When should I hire a fractional sales director instead of a full-time one?

When you need senior sales leadership now but the business is not yet at the revenue level or team size that justifies a full-time hire. A full-time sales director costs significantly more once you include salary, bonus, equity and on-costs, and takes months to hire and ramp. A fractional director starts quickly, brings patterns from other businesses and is far easier to adjust if the scope changes.

How long does a fractional sales director engagement last?

Most engagements run three to nine months. Long enough to build the process, coach the team and hand over a working system. Some businesses taper to a lighter retainer afterwards for quarterly planning and oversight. The engagement ends when the function can run without regular senior input, either because the team manages itself or because a full-time hire takes over.
