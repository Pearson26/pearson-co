Lead management and routing setup: get every inbound lead worked properly.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.23 June 20268 min read

A lead management process is the set of rules, CRM configuration and workflows that govern what happens to a lead from the moment it arrives: how it is scored, who it is routed to, when they must respond, and what happens if it is not yet ready to buy. Without this setup, leads fall through the gaps regardless of how good the acquisition is.

Most B2B teams I speak with do not have a traffic problem. They have a plumbing problem. Inbound leads are coming in from paid ads, content, events or partnerships, and then almost nothing happens with them in any consistent way. A lead lands in the CRM or, worse, in a shared inbox. Someone may pick it up, or they may not. Response times vary from two hours to two days depending on who is in the office. Marketing calls it a qualified lead; sales calls it junk. Nobody agrees on what a good lead even looks like. The lead management process is the work of fixing that: defining the rules, building them into the CRM, and making the whole path from form submission to first sales conversation predictable and fast.

This page covers what the setup engagement includes, who it is designed for, and what you should expect when we are done. If you want to talk through your current setup first, book a call and bring your CRM and your conversion numbers.

What a lead management process actually is.

The term covers five connected things, and you need all five for the system to hold together. Miss any one of them and the others break down.

First: a shared definition of what counts as a lead worth working. Marketing and sales almost always disagree on this until someone forces the conversation. The marketing qualified lead (MQL) definition, or the BANT criteria your sales team uses, needs to be written down, agreed, and built into the CRM so the handoff is automatic rather than a negotiation.

Second: lead scoring. Not a complex model with twenty weighted fields, but a practical scoring layer that separates leads worth calling today from those worth a nurture sequence. I set up two dimensions: explicit scoring (firmographic data: company size, industry, job title, geography) and implicit scoring (behavioural signals: page views, content downloads, pricing page visits, email engagement). Explicit scoring tells you who they are; implicit scoring tells you what they are interested in right now.

Third: routing rules. A lead that lands in a general queue and waits for someone to claim it is a lead that goes cold. The routing logic determines who it goes to, immediately, based on territory, account ownership, product line, or round-robin assignment across a team. This is configured in the CRM, not in a process document that nobody reads.

Fourth: response SLAs. Harvard Business Review research by Oldroyd et al. (2011) found that companies contacting a lead within one hour were nearly seven times more likely to qualify it than those who waited longer. That gap is not about persistence; it is about relevance. The lead is thinking about your product at the moment they submit. An hour later they are thinking about something else. An SLA without enforcement is just a number, so the setup includes automated alerts and task creation that make it impossible to miss a lead without a notification firing.

Fifth: a nurture path for not-ready leads. A meaningful share of inbound leads are genuinely interested but not ready to buy this month. Without a structured nurture flow, those leads either get passed to sales too early (and rejected), or they sit in the CRM until someone deletes them at year end. The engagement sets up the handoff from your marketing automation tool into the CRM, and configures the nurture sequences that keep those leads warm until they cross the scoring threshold.

Who this is for.

This setup engagement is for B2B founders and revenue leaders who have one or more of the following problems. You have inbound leads coming in but no reliable way to know which ones get followed up, by whom, and within what timeframe. Your CRM has a lead object but it is inconsistently filled in and sales reps work from their inbox instead of the pipeline. You have a marketing automation tool (HubSpot, Marketo, ActiveCampaign, or similar) that is generating leads but the handoff to sales is manual or entirely broken. Your close rate is lower than it should be and you suspect that slow or inconsistent follow-up is a meaningful part of the reason.

It is also for teams preparing to scale. If you are about to hire two or three salespeople, getting the routing and qualification logic right before they start means they are working a structured queue on day one rather than fighting over who owns which lead. That conversation gets expensive quickly at scale.

This is a CRM and process setup engagement, not a coaching programme. The output is configuration: rules, scoring models, workflows, and documented SOPs that your team can run. If you need broader work on the CRM itself, this connects directly to CRM implementation and CRM consulting. What is included.

Every engagement is scoped to your CRM, your marketing automation setup, and your team structure. The consistent deliverables are below.

  • Lead definition workshop. A working session with marketing and sales to agree the MQL and SQL definitions, the BANT or equivalent qualification criteria, and the rules that move a lead from one stage to the next. This document becomes the source of truth the CRM is built from.
  • Lead scoring model. A two-dimension scoring matrix covering explicit firmographic fit and implicit behavioural engagement, configured in your CRM or marketing automation platform. Thresholds are set for MQL handoff and for nurture entry, and the model is documented so it can be updated when your ICP shifts.
  • Routing rules and assignment logic. Built and tested in the CRM: territory-based, round-robin, or account-based routing (or a combination), with fallback rules for unassigned leads and owner-away handling. Every inbound lead gets an owner within minutes of arriving.
  • Response SLA configuration. Automated task creation, internal alerts and, where the CRM supports it, escalation rules. The aim is to make a missed SLA visible immediately rather than discoverable in a weekly report.
  • Marketing-to-CRM handoff. The technical connection between your marketing automation tool and the CRM, including field mapping, data normalisation and the suppression lists that stop contacts already in the pipeline from being re-enrolled in top-of-funnel sequences.
  • Nurture flow structure for not-ready leads. A mapped and configured nurture programme for leads below the MQL threshold, with re-scoring triggers so a lead that becomes active re-enters the routing workflow automatically rather than requiring a manual review.
  • SOPs and handoff documentation. Written process maps covering what happens at each stage, what the rep's responsibilities are, and how to handle the edge cases (duplicates, inbound from an existing customer, high-value leads that skip the queue).

The engagement does not include writing email copy for the nurture sequences (that is a separate content brief), but it does include the workflow logic, timing rules, and branching that the copy slots into.

How the lead management setup engagement works.

The engagement runs in three phases. The first is discovery: a structured audit of your current lead flow, from the form or inbound source through to what actually happens in the CRM. I look at where leads come from, what data arrives with them, how they are currently assigned (formally or informally), what the existing scoring or qualification logic is (if any), and where leads go when they are not ready. Most audits surface two or three places where leads are simply disappearing: no owner, no follow-up task, no sequence.

The second phase is build. I configure the scoring model, routing rules, SLAs and nurture flows in your CRM and marketing automation tool. This is done live in your systems, not in a slide deck. Where the CRM needs data fields it does not currently have (lead source, product interest, company size), I map and create them. Where there are existing workflows that conflict with the new routing logic, I document the decision about whether to replace or preserve them.

The third phase is handover and testing. We run the full flow with real and test leads, check that routing fires correctly, that scoring increments on behavioural activity, and that the nurture entry trigger works as specified. I produce the SOP documentation and run a short session with the sales and marketing leads to walk them through what changed and what they need to do differently. You should expect the engagement to take two to four weeks from kick-off to signed-off handover, depending on the complexity of your CRM setup and how many source systems feed leads in.

Outcomes and proof.

The primary outcome is a working lead management process: every inbound lead gets an owner, a response time, and a next action, automatically. That is the baseline. Beyond that, the specific numbers that shift depend on your starting point, but these are the consistent patterns I see after this engagement.

Response times drop sharply, usually from a multi-day average to same-day or sub-hour, once the routing fires automatically and the SLA task creation removes any ambiguity about who is responsible. That alone tends to improve qualification rates, because the Harvard Business Review finding about seven times higher qualification within the first hour holds across B2B categories: intent is perishable.

MQL-to-SQL conversion improves because the definition is agreed and enforced in the CRM rather than left to individual interpretation. Sales stops receiving leads it considers unqualified. Marketing stops hearing that its leads are junk. That is not just a morale improvement: it means the pipeline is built from leads that have a realistic chance of converting, and the forecast becomes more trustworthy as a result.

The nurture pool becomes an asset rather than a write-off. Leads that were previously rejected or ignored because they were not ready get a structured sequence, a re-scoring mechanism, and a clear re-entry point into the sales workflow. For B2B businesses with longer buying cycles, that pool is often worth more in the next quarter than the new inbound that month.

If you are preparing to scale the sales team, the process also means that a new rep sits down on day one to a structured queue with clear ownership rules, rather than spending the first two weeks arguing with existing reps about who owns which account. That onboarding efficiency is a real benefit that is easy to undervalue when things are running slowly and easy to notice acutely when they are not.

Common questions.

What is the difference between lead scoring and lead routing?

Lead scoring determines how ready and how well-suited a lead is, based on firmographic fit and behavioural signals. Lead routing determines who receives the lead once it meets the threshold to be worked. Scoring answers the question of whether to act. Routing answers the question of who acts. Both need to be configured in the CRM for the system to run without manual intervention.

Which CRMs do you work with for lead management setup?

Most commonly HubSpot, Salesforce and Pipedrive, which cover the majority of B2B teams I work with. The logic, scoring model and SOP structure are the same regardless of platform. The specific configuration steps vary, and I scope the engagement to your actual CRM once we have had an initial call.

How long does it take to respond to a lead before it goes cold?

Research from Harvard Business Review (Oldroyd et al., 2011) found that companies contacting leads within one hour were nearly seven times more likely to qualify them than those who waited. For most B2B categories the practical target is same-day at a minimum, sub-hour for high-intent signals like a pricing page visit followed by a form submission. The SLA configuration in this engagement is designed to make that achievable without relying on someone remembering to check a queue.

What if we do not have a marketing automation tool yet?

The lead scoring and routing setup can still be built in the CRM using native workflows and lead source data. You lose some of the behavioural scoring that a marketing automation tool provides, but the routing logic, SLA enforcement and qualification criteria are entirely CRM-native and do not require a separate platform. If you are considering adding a marketing automation tool, that decision is part of the discovery conversation.

Is this the same as a CRM implementation?

No. A CRM implementation sets up the platform: the objects, pipelines, users, integrations and data structure. A lead management setup works within an existing CRM to configure the specific rules, scoring, routing and workflows that govern how leads are handled. Often the two happen together if the CRM was implemented without the lead logic, but they are distinct scopes of work.

What happens to leads that never reach the MQL threshold?

They go into a nurture path with a re-scoring trigger. When a lead's implicit score increases because they visit the pricing page, open a sequence of emails, or attend a webinar, the system re-evaluates their score and routes them into the sales workflow automatically. Without that mechanism, not-ready leads either get deleted or sit in the CRM as dead weight. The nurture configuration is included in this engagement.
