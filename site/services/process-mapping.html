Operations & process

Process mapping that finds your bottlenecks.

Lauren PearsonFounder, The Pearson Co.16 June 202610 min read

Process mapping draws out how a piece of work really moves through your business, step by step, from the trigger that starts it to the outcome that ends it. Laid out end to end, it shows the bottlenecks, the handoffs and the rework that slow things down, so you can fix the flow rather than guess at it.

Most growing businesses run on processes nobody ever wrote down. The work gets done, mostly, but the way it gets done lives in a handful of people's heads and a long chain of emails. Then a deal sits for a week because nobody knew it was theirs to move. An invoice goes out wrong. A new hire takes three months to be useful because there's no clear way to show them how anything actually works. Everyone is busy and nothing is obviously broken, yet things keep slipping. That gap between how you think the business runs and how it really runs is the gap process mapping closes.

A process map is a picture of how work flows from start to finish: every step, in order, with the person or system that owns it and the points where it waits. Drawn out honestly, the friction becomes obvious. The approval that takes three days. The handoff between sales and finance where things go quiet. The same data typed into two systems by two different people. These stop being vague frustrations and become specific things you can fix.

This page explains what process mapping is, why it pays for itself, and how I run it so the map becomes practical improvement rather than a diagram nobody opens again.

What process mapping actually is.

A process map is a visual record of the steps a piece of work passes through, in the order they happen, with the detail that makes the flow real: who does each step, what system it touches, what triggers it, and where it waits or loops back. The level of detail depends on the question you're trying to answer, but the spine is always the same: a clear start, a clear end, and every step in between laid out so you can see the whole thing at once.

It is not an org chart. An org chart shows who reports to whom. A process map shows how work actually moves across those boxes, which is usually nothing like the reporting lines. The work cuts sideways through the business: a single order might pass through sales, operations, finance and support before it's done. The map follows that path, which is exactly why it surfaces problems an org chart hides.

It is also not a one-off documentation project. The best process map is a working picture that reflects how things are done today and gets revisited when the team, the tools or the volume change. A flowchart drawn once and filed away is just decoration. The value is in what the map shows you and what you do about it.

Why process mapping matters commercially.

The reason to map a process is money and time, not neatness. Every bottleneck is a place where work, and often revenue, sits waiting. Every handoff with no clear owner is a place where things get dropped. Every duplicated step is paid effort producing nothing. You can't price any of that until you can see it laid out, and once you can, the cost is usually larger than anyone guessed.

A few things tend to surface the moment a process is mapped honestly:

  • The bottleneck nobody sized. One step that everything queues behind, often an approval or a single overloaded person. Find it and the whole process speeds up.
  • The handoff nobody owns. Sales thinks operations has it, operations thinks finance has it, and the work sits in the gap. The seams between teams are where most processes break.
  • The rework you keep paying for. The same information entered twice, the checks done because an earlier step can't be trusted, the chasing for an answer. Every bit of that is cost with no output.

For a scaling or SaaS business this compounds fast. A process that just about works at ten people quietly breaks at fifty, because the informal glue that held it together stops scaling. Mapping it before you add headcount or change systems means you grow on something solid instead of cementing the chaos. It pairs naturally with revenue operations, where the maps become the backbone of how the whole revenue engine runs, and it feeds directly into standard operating procedures once you know which steps are worth keeping.

How to create a process map: the way I run it.

Here is how I approach business process mapping in a client engagement. It's deliberately practical. The aim is a map you can act on within weeks, not a wall of flowcharts that takes a quarter to draw and changes nothing.

1. Pick one process and bound it

A map that tries to cover everything covers nothing well. We start with a single process that matters, for example lead to first invoice, or new-client onboarding, or a support ticket from raised to resolved. We agree exactly where it starts and where it ends. One process, one clear boundary. We map the others later.

2. Walk it with the people who actually do the work

The official process and the real process are rarely the same. So we don't map from a policy document; we sit with the people doing the work and walk through what really happens, step by step. The workarounds, the shortcuts, the "oh, and then I usually just email Sarah" moments: those are the gold. A map of how things are supposed to work hides every problem worth finding.

3. Capture each step with its owner and its wait

For every step we record four things: what happens, who does it, which system it touches, and how long it waits before the next step picks it up. That last one matters more than people expect. Most of the time a process takes isn't work; it's waiting, sitting in someone's queue. Mapping the waits is how you find where the days actually go.

4. Bring in real data, not just memory

Where I can, I attach real numbers to the map: how many items flow through each month, how long each stage really takes, where deals or tickets pile up. Your CRM and your other systems hold a lot of this if you know where to look. The team's memory tells you what feels slow. The data tells you what is slow, and the two often disagree. Getting this right usually depends on your systems holding clean, joined-up records, which is why mapping so often surfaces a CRM implementation or data-quality problem sitting underneath the process.

5. Mark the bottlenecks, handoffs and rework

With the flow laid out and the data attached, the problems show themselves. We mark each bottleneck, each ownerless handoff, each duplicated or unnecessary step, and put a rough cost against it: time, money, or risk. That number is what turns a long list of annoyances into a ranked set of priorities. Not everything clunky is worth fixing first.

6. Turn the map into a fix list with owners

This is the step that separates a useful map from a poster. Every priority problem becomes a specific change, with a named owner and a date. Remove the approval that adds nothing. Give the orphaned handoff a clear owner. Cut the second system the data was being keyed into. The map is the diagnosis. The fix list is the treatment, and that's the part most exercises skip.

7. Make the improvement stick

Finally we connect the fixes to how the business runs day to day, so the improvements hold instead of decaying back to the old way. That usually means writing the surviving steps up as clear procedures, and often setting up CRM automation to handle the prompts, routing and follow-ups that people were doing by hand and forgetting. A map that changes how the team works is worth far more than one that changes how a slide looks.

What a finished map looks like.

People picture a process map as a sprawling flowchart with dozens of boxes and arrows. In practice the useful version is closer to a table: one row per step, in order, with columns for what happens, who owns it, the system it touches, and where it stalls. Here's a stripped-back example for a lead-to-invoice process, so you can see the shape of it.

InvoiceFirst invoice raised and sentFinanceWaits days for operations to confirm it's live

Each stall in that table is a number you can size and a fix you can own. That's the whole point: the map is the diagnosis, laid out so the next move is obvious.

The mistakes that waste the exercise.

I get called in as often to rescue a stalled mapping effort as to start one. The same few mistakes come up:

  • Mapping the ideal process instead of the real one. A map of how you wish work happened hides every problem worth finding. Walk the real path with the people who do it.
  • Stopping at the diagram. A tidy flowchart with no fix list, no owners and no dates changes nothing. The output is improvement, not a picture.
  • Mapping in too much detail too soon. A first map at the wrong altitude buries the big problems under tiny ones. Start at the level where the bottlenecks are visible, then zoom in only where it pays to.
  • Doing it without the people on the tools. Map a process from a manager's office and you'll document a fiction. The people doing the work know where it really breaks.

Process mapping and the bigger picture.

Process mapping rarely stays a standalone exercise, because the moment you can see how work flows, you can see what to do next with it. A clean map is the input to almost everything else in operations. It tells you which steps to write up as standard operating procedures so the work is repeatable. It shows you which steps are ripe for automation and which need a human, so you automate the right things rather than just the easy ones. And it sits alongside customer journey mapping: the journey map follows the customer's experience, while the process map follows the work behind the scenes that delivers it. Where the two cross is usually where customers feel the friction, the slow reply, the dropped handoff, the thing that fell through a gap.

So the order matters. Map first, so you understand the flow. Fix the obvious waste. Then document and automate what's left, on top of a process that actually works. Automating a broken process just lets you make the same mistakes faster.

From map to improvement.

A process map is only worth drawing if it changes how the work gets done. The version I run is built to do exactly that: take one process, walk it as it really happens, ground it in real data, find the bottlenecks and handoffs that cost you time and money, and hand you a short list of fixes with names and dates against them. Common questions.

What is process mapping?

Process mapping is the practice of drawing out how a piece of work actually moves through a business, step by step, from the trigger that starts it to the outcome that ends it. It records each task, who does it, what system it touches, and where it waits. Laid out end to end, it shows the bottlenecks, the handoffs and the rework that slow things down and cost money.

How do you create a process map?

Pick one process and define where it starts and ends. Walk through it with the people who actually do the work and write down each step in order, noting who owns it, what system it uses, and how long it waits between steps. Map the real process, not the official one. Then mark the bottlenecks, the handoffs and the duplicated effort, and turn the worst of them into a short list of fixes with owners and dates.

What is the difference between a process map and an SOP?

A process map shows the flow: the sequence of steps, who hands off to whom, and where work stalls. A standard operating procedure is the written instruction for doing one of those steps well. The map is the diagnosis of how work moves; the SOP is how you make a given step repeatable once you've decided it's worth keeping. You usually map first, then document the steps that survive.

What tools do I need for business process mapping?

Far less than people expect. A whiteboard, sticky notes, or a shared board in Miro, FigJam or Lucidchart is enough for a first map. The thinking and the honesty matter more than the software. What does matter is pulling real timing and volume data from your CRM and your tools, so the map reflects what happens rather than what the org chart says should happen.

When should a business invest in process mapping?

When growth has outpaced the way work gets done. The usual signals are the same tasks done differently by different people, things falling through the cracks between teams, a few key people who are the only ones who know how something works, and onboarding that takes too long. If you're about to scale headcount, change systems or enter a new market, mapping first saves you from automating a broken process.

How long does a process mapping engagement take?

A single, well-bounded process can be mapped and turned into a ranked fix list in one to three weeks, depending on how many people touch it and how clean your data is. Mapping a whole function, such as the full lead-to-cash flow, takes longer. The aim is always a map you can act on within weeks, not a documentation project that runs for months.
